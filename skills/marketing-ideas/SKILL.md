# Marketing Ideas Generation Skill

## Mandatory Content Standards

- Write between 1,500 and 10,000 words per skill output.
- Write in a way that sounds like a knowledgeable human wrote it. No robotic or templated phrasing.
- Use short sentences. One idea per sentence. One focus per paragraph.
- Use active voice. Never passive constructions.
- Address the reader directly using "you" and "your."
- Use bullet points only when they genuinely improve readability.
- Replace all em dashes with commas, parentheses, semicólons, or a new sentence. No hidden Unicode characters.
- End every sentence with a period.
- No hashtags, emojis, or asterisks.
- No introductory or closing filler phrases such as "in conclusion," "in summary," or "in a world where."
- No warnings, notes, or disclaimers. Stick to requested output.
- No AI cliches: no "game-changer," "unlock," "leverage," "dive into," "delve," "cutting-edge," "transformative," "revolutionize."
- No excessive adjectives or adverbs. Let specifics do the work.
- No broad generalizations. Every claim tied to specific context.
- Use specific examples, data, and scenarios.
- Pose at least one thought-provoking question per skill.
- Mobile-friendly: short paragraphs, clear headers, scannable.
- Practical and actionable. Every section connects to a next step.

## What This Skill Does

This skill helps you generate, evaluate, and prioritize marketing ideas for SaaS and software products. It covers channel-specific ideation, underused tactics, growth loops, content strategy, community approaches, partnership models, and how to structure a marketing experiment backlog so you always know what to run next.

Most SaaS companies spend 90% of their marketing effort on 20% of the available channels, because those channels feel familiar. The ideas that can actually move the needle are often the ones that feel slightly uncomfortable or unusual.

The question that should drive your ideation: what do you know about your customers that your competitors do not know about their customers, and how could that knowledge produce a marketing approach no one else is running?

## Part 1: The Brainstorming Framework

Before you generate ideas, define the constraints. Unconstrained brainstorming produces interesting ideas that cannot be acted on. Constrained brainstorming produces ideas you can actually implement.

### Define the Constraints First

Before a brainstorm session, write down:

- Your current monthly marketing budget (rough range is fine).
- Your current team capacity (how many people-hours per week can go toward new experiments).
- Your primary channel goals for the quarter (traffic, signups, revenue, brand awareness).
- Your ICP (the exact person you are trying to reach).
- What you have already tried and the results.

These constraints are not limitations. They are the conditions that make ideas useful rather than theoretical.

### The Brainstorming Approach

Generate ideas in clusters, not as a single undifferentiated list.

**Cluster 1: Channel ideas.** What new channels have you not explored? Where does your ICP spend time that you are not present?

**Cluster 2: Content ideas.** What could you create (articles, videos, tools, reports, data) that your ICP would find valuable enough to share?

**Cluster 3: Partnership ideas.** Who already serves your ICP and is not a competitor? What could you do together?

**Cluster 4: Community ideas.** Where are your customers talking to each other? How could you facilitate or support that?

**Cluster 5: Product-led ideas.** What features or behaviors in your product create natural sharing or virality?

**Cluster 6: Offers and promotions.** What pricing structure, trial offer, or promotion could reduce friction for a specific audience?

Generate five to ten ideas per cluster before evaluating any of them. Evaluation kills generative thinking.

## Part 2: Underused Marketing Channels

The most competitive channels attract the most marketing teams. Underused channels often produce better results precisely because competition is lower.

### Podcast Guesting (Not Starting a Podcast)

Starting a podcast is a long-term brand play. Guesting on other podcasts is a near-term lead generation play.

Identify the 30 to 50 podcasts your ICP listens to. Many of these are niche shows with 1,000 to 10,000 listeners. The audience is small but highly aligned. Reach out to hosts with a specific, valuable topic you can discuss. Do not pitch your product. Pitch a conversation your audience would find useful.

A relevant guest interview on a niche podcast with 3,000 loyal listeners often drives more qualified signups than a guest post on a publication with 300,000 casual readers.

### Newsletter Sponsorships and Swap Deals

Newsletters in your niche have already done the work of building an audience. Sponsoring a relevant newsletter puts your message in front of a curated, engaged audience.

The most cost-effective approach: newsletter swap deals with non-competing products that serve the same audience. You mention their product to your list. They mention your product to theirs. No money changes hands. This works when both lists are roughly comparable in size and quality.

Paid newsletter sponsorships work when the newsletter's audience closely matches your ICP and the cost per click is below your acceptable customer acquisition cost threshold.

### Community-Led Marketing

Instead of building a community (which takes 12 to 24 months to gain momentum), participate in existing communities where your ICP already gathers.

The approach: answer questions, contribute resources, and be helpful consistently. Over six months, you become known as an expert. When someone in the community needs a solution, they think of you first.

This is slow and not easily scaled, but the quality of relationships and referrals it produces is unusually high. It works best for niche products with a defined professional audience.

### Cold LinkedIn Outreach (Organic, Not Paid)

LinkedIn's organic reach for personal profiles is significantly higher than for company pages. A personal LinkedIn post from the founder of a SaaS company regularly outperforms the company page's content by 5 to 10 times.

The outreach version: use LinkedIn's Sales Navigator or basic search to find people who match your ICP by title, industry, and company size. Connect with a brief, specific note that references their work. Follow up with value: a resource, a relevant article, or a question. Do not pitch in the first message.

This approach works when your product has a high average contract value that justifies the time investment per prospect.

### Building in Public

Document your company's growth and product decisions publicly. Share specific metrics: revenue milestones, marketing experiments and their results, product decisions and the reasoning behind them.

Building in public on Twitter, LinkedIn, or a public newsletter attracts founders, operators, and practitioners who are exactly the type of person who evaluates and purchases SaaS tools. It creates a free audience of warm prospects who know your product intimately before they sign up.

The risk: sharing metrics invites competitors to learn from your experiments. The benefit: the audience and trust you build typically outweighs this.

### Directory-Based SEO

Creating and maintaining a public directory in your category builds long-term organic traffic. A "Best [category] Tools" directory page on your site can rank for high-intent queries and attract backlinks from other content creators.

The approach: build a structured directory of tools in your category (including your competitors), keep it updated, and make it genuinely useful. Visitors find you through the directory content. You appear as an expert and a participant in the category, not just a vendor.

### Data-Driven Reports and Studies

Publishing original research generates sustained backlink and press attention in a way few other content types match.

The approach: survey your customers or industry peers on a specific question. Publish the results with analysis. Pitch the data to journalists and newsletter authors. Original data is one of the easiest things to pitch to press because journalists need it and rarely have access to it.

An annual "State of [Your Category]" report, produced consistently, becomes a destination resource that earns backlinks year after year.

## Part 3: Content Marketing Ideas

Content marketing generates long-term compounding returns. The caveat: most content does not rank, does not get read, and does not generate leads. The ideas that work are specific, targeted, and built around actual search demand or audience interest.

### SEO Content Ideas That Actually Work

**Competitor comparison pages:** "Our Product vs. Competitor A" and "Our Product vs. Competitor B." These pages rank for searches that people make when they are actively comparing vendors. Conversion rates are very high because the visitor has high purchase intent.

**Alternative pages:** "Best Alternatives to [Competitor]" pages. These capture people who are frustrated with a competitor and looking for something different. Write these as genuinely helpful guides, not pure promotional content.

**Use case pages:** "[Product] for [specific role or industry]." A project management tool might have separate pages for "project management for marketing teams," "project management for agencies," and "project management for remote teams." Each page targets a different search query and a different segment.

**Question-based content:** Write articles that directly answer the questions your ICP types into Google. Identify these questions using Google's "People Also Ask" section, Answer the Public, or Ahrefs. Answer the question completely and specifically in the first two paragraphs.

**Tutorial content:** "How to [accomplish specific outcome]" articles. These rank well, attract people who are actively trying to solve a problem, and can naturally reference your product as part of the solution.

### Non-SEO Content Ideas

**LinkedIn essays:** Long-form posts on LinkedIn about specific problems in your category. Not thought leadership about trends. Practical, specific content that solves a real problem. A well-written LinkedIn post from a founder can reach 10,000 to 100,000 people in a niche audience with no paid promotion.

**Twitter / X threads:** A detailed breakdown of a specific topic, presented as a thread with concrete examples and takeaways. Threads that provide genuine value get shared by people with relevant audiences.

**Email newsletter:** A weekly or biweekly email newsletter for your category that is genuinely useful independent of your product. People subscribe because the content is valuable. They become familiar with your brand and more likely to consider your product when a relevant need arises.

**Video walkthroughs:** Screen-recorded walkthroughs of how to accomplish specific tasks relevant to your product's category. Published on YouTube, these can rank for video searches and embed naturally in your own blog content.

## Part 4: Growth Loops

A growth loop is a marketing mechanic where the output of one cycle becomes the input of the next. Unlike a funnel (which runs once per customer), a loop compounds.

### Common Growth Loop Types

**Content loop:** You publish content. Content attracts visitors. Visitors sign up. Customers provide case studies and testimonials. Case studies attract more visitors. The output (customer stories) feeds the input (content marketing).

**Referral loop:** Customers refer friends and colleagues. Referred customers also refer. Each wave of customers generates the next wave through word of mouth. This loop requires a product people are enthusiastic about using.

**Viral product loop:** The product itself triggers sharing. A tool that generates a shareable output (a report, a graphic, a score) places your branding on the shared content. Viewers of the shared content discover your product. Canva's watermarking strategy is the canonical example.

**Community loop:** You build or support a community. Community members share knowledge. New members join because the community is valuable. More members make the community more valuable. More value attracts more members.

**SEO loop:** You publish content that ranks. Rankings generate traffic. Traffic generates backlinks (people link to content they find useful). Backlinks improve rankings. Higher rankings generate more traffic.

### Building a Growth Loop

To build a growth loop, answer these three questions:

1. What does a user do inside your product that naturally creates value for someone else?
2. What would motivate that person to share what they created?
3. How does that sharing bring new people back to your product?

If you cannot answer all three, you do not have a loop yet. Keep looking.

## Part 5: Virality Mechanics

Virality is not luck. It is a designed property that some products have and others do not. You can increase your product's viral coefficient by building specific mechanics.

### Types of Virality

**Inherent virality:** The product only works when shared. Collaborative tools (Figma, Notion, Slack) require inviting other users. Every invitation is a distribution event.

**Exposed virality:** The product creates output that the user shares externally. The output carries your brand. "Created with [Tool]" watermarks on Canva designs. "Sent via [Email Tool]" footers. The share is part of normal product use.

**Incentivized virality:** The user shares because sharing benefits them. Dropbox's referral program (more storage for referrals) increased signups by 60% in its early days. The incentive must be directly related to product value.

**Word-of-mouth virality:** The product is so good or so distinctive that people mention it in conversations. This is the hardest type to design for and the most durable when it occurs.

### Diagnosing Your Viral Coefficient

Your viral coefficient is the average number of new users each existing user generates. A coefficient above 1 means the product grows without any external marketing. A coefficient below 1 means you need marketing inputs to grow.

Most SaaS products have a viral coefficient between 0.1 and 0.4. This means marketing is still the primary growth driver, but word of mouth meaningfully reduces the cost of growth.

To improve your viral coefficient:
- Make sharing easier (reduce friction to invite, share, or reference the product).
- Increase the user's motivation to share (improve product delight, add referral incentives).
- Ensure the shared content or invitation converts well (improve the landing page for invited users).

## Part 6: Community Tactics

Community is a long game. But specific community tactics produce results on shorter timelines.

### Infiltration (Not Promotion)

Join five to ten communities where your ICP spends time. Spend the first 30 days only consuming and observing. Note the recurring questions, the language people use, the tools they mention.

At day 30, begin contributing. Answer questions with specific, useful answers. Do not mention your product unless directly relevant. Over 60 to 90 days, you become a recognized contributor.

After 90 days, when you share something about your product, the community receives it well because they know you as a helpful person, not a promoter.

### Hosting Events and Workshops

Online workshops and webinars bring your ICP together around a specific topic. They generate leads, establish expertise, and produce content (recordings) that you can repurpose.

The most effective workshops are:
- Specific in topic (not "intro to marketing" but "how to calculate CAC for a freemium SaaS").
- Practical and hands-on (participants apply something during the session).
- Limited in attendance (creates exclusivity and quality of engagement).

Invite a guest who brings their own audience. A workshop with a respected practitioner attracts their followers and exposes your brand to a new audience.

### Building a Slack or Discord Community

Building your own community is a 12 to 24 month investment before it becomes self-sustaining. It is appropriate when:
- Your audience already wants to connect with each other.
- You can provide consistent programming (AMAs, weekly discussions, expert guests).
- Your team has capacity to moderate and facilitate.

A community that serves members first and promotes your product second retains members and compounds trust. A community that feels like a marketing channel loses members.

## Part 7: Partnership Ideas

Partnerships extend your reach without requiring you to build an audience from scratch.

### Integration Partnerships

Integrate your product with tools your customers already use. Each integration creates mutual visibility. The partner's documentation, app store, or integration directory lists your product. Their customers discover you.

Integrations also reduce churn: customers who use your product alongside multiple other tools have a higher switching cost.

Prioritize integrations with tools that:
- Your customers mention using in interviews.
- Have large, active app directories.
- Serve your ICP without competing with you.

### Affiliate and Referral Partnerships

Create an affiliate program that rewards partners (bloggers, consultants, agency owners, newsletter authors) for referring customers. A well-run affiliate program can become a significant acquisition channel.

The mechanics: track referrals with unique links. Pay a commission on first payment, a recurring commission, or a flat fee per signup. Pay out reliably and promptly. Affiliates leave programs that pay slowly or inconsistently.

The best affiliate partners are people who already recommend tools in your category to their audience. A consultant who advises clients on marketing tech is a natural partner for a marketing SaaS.

### Co-Marketing with Non-Competing Tools

Partner with a non-competing product that serves the same audience. Co-produce a piece of content (a joint report, a webinar, a guide), co-sponsor an event, or run a joint promotion.

Each partner promotes the co-created asset to their respective audiences. Both parties gain access to the other's audience without a monetary exchange.

## Part 8: Prioritizing Marketing Experiments

Every team has more marketing ideas than time. Prioritization is the skill that determines which ideas you actually run.

### The Effort-Impact Framework

Score each marketing idea on two dimensions:

**Effort:** How much time and money does this require? Score 1 to 5. 1 is very low effort (a few hours). 5 is very high effort (weeks of work, significant budget).

**Potential impact:** How much could this move the metrics that matter most to you? Score 1 to 5. 1 is minimal impact (a few visitors or leads). 5 is significant impact (material change in signups or revenue).

Calculate a priority score: impact divided by effort. Ideas with a score above 1.5 are high-priority experiments. Ideas with a score below 0.5 are low-priority.

This framework surfaces the high-impact, low-effort ideas that should run immediately. It also surfaces the high-effort, low-impact ideas that should be deprioritized regardless of how interesting they feel.

### The Experiment Backlog

Maintain a living backlog of marketing ideas in a shared document or Airtable. For each idea, record:

- The hypothesis ("If we publish a weekly roundup of industry news, it will grow our email list by X% in 90 days").
- The channel or tactic.
- The effort score.
- The potential impact score.
- The priority score.
- The next action required to start.
- The owner.
- The status (not started, in progress, live, complete, archived).

Review the backlog weekly. Move one to three ideas from "not started" to "in progress" each sprint. Archive ideas that no longer fit your strategy without deleting them. Revisiting archived ideas at a later stage often reveals timing-dependent opportunities.

### How to Run a Marketing Experiment

For each experiment:

1. Write the hypothesis before starting.
2. Define the success criteria (what number, over what time period, would confirm this works).
3. Set a minimum run time (most experiments need at least 30 days to produce meaningful data).
4. Run it. Do not tweak it during the test.
5. After the minimum run time, evaluate against the success criteria.
6. Decide: scale, iterate, or archive.

The discipline of running experiments this way prevents premature abandonment (stopping a good idea before it has time to work) and sunk-cost continuation (keeping a bad idea running because you invested in it).

### The Compounding Experiment Advantage

Teams that run 20 well-structured experiments per year outperform teams that run 5 large campaigns per year, even if individual experiments fail more often. Each experiment produces either a channel to scale or a lesson to apply.

Over time, the experiments that work compound. A content strategy that starts generating 100 monthly visitors compounds to 10,000 over 24 months if you keep publishing. A referral program that generates 5 referrals per month compounds to a meaningful acquisition channel over two years.

Start experiments sooner than feels comfortable. The results will tell you whether to continue faster than any amount of planning will.
