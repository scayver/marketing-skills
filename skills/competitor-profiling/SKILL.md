## What This Skill Does

This skill researches a competitor and produces a structured dossier. The output is not a list of features. It is an intelligence document your team can use to sharpen positioning, prepare for sales conversations, brief new employees, and make product decisions.

A good competitor profile answers: who they are going after, what they say about themselves, what customers say about them, how they make money, and where they are weak.

When given a competitor URL or name, this skill examines the website, product pages, pricing, messaging, customer reviews, job postings, content, social presence, and any available traffic or funding data, then organizes those findings into a structured document.

---

## Why Competitor Research Is Worth Doing Rigorously

Most companies know their competitors exist. Fewer actually understand them in detail. Knowing a competitor's name and vague feature set is not competitive intelligence. It is just awareness.

Genuine competitor research tells you things like: who they have decided not to serve, what language their customers use to describe their problems, which market segments they are moving into, and what they are struggling to retain customers on. Those details shape better messaging, better positioning, and better sales conversations.

Here is a question to sit with: if your best salesperson had 60 minutes to prepare for a call where the prospect just said they were also evaluating a specific competitor, what would they actually want to know?

That is the standard for a useful competitor profile.

## What to Research

A complete competitor profile covers ten areas. Some are easier to find than others. All of them matter.

### 1. Positioning and Core Message

Start with the homepage. Read the headline, the subheadline, and the first 200 words of the page. These are not random. They represent a deliberate choice about who the company is talking to and what it most wants to be known for.

Note:

- The primary headline and the claim it makes
- Who the homepage visually and verbally addresses (role, company size, industry)
- What problem they lead with versus what product they lead with
- Whether they position against a category (versus a spreadsheet, versus the status quo) or against specific competitors
- What proof they use on the homepage (customer logos, stats, quotes)

Then visit the "About" page, the careers page, and any mission or values page. These often contain language that reveals how the company thinks about its market and its differentiation.

### 2. Target Customer

Look for explicit signals of who they serve. Some companies are transparent about their ICP. Others hide it in the details.

Sources:

- Customer logos on the homepage or customers page. Are they enterprise, mid-market, or SMB? What industries appear?
- Testimonials. What role is the person who gave the quote? What did they say the product solved for them?
- Case studies. Who are the detailed success stories? What was the situation before, and what changed?
- Pricing page. Does the pricing structure imply a particular company size? Is there a free tier (SMB signal) or is pricing enterprise-negotiated?
- Blog and content topics. What problems do they write about? Who do those problems belong to?
- LinkedIn follower profile. If you can see the audience breakdown for their LinkedIn page, job titles and company sizes in that audience reveal who they reach.

### 3. Product Features

Read every product page. List the primary features and how they are described. Note the language used for each feature, not just the feature itself. The language tells you what benefit they believe buyers care about.

Do not just copy their feature list. Note which features get the most real estate on the page. Prominence on a product page usually reflects the features the company believes close deals.

Also check:

- What integrations they advertise (these reveal what ecosystem they are building for)
- What they put in the primary navigation versus what they bury in the footer
- Whether they have a public product roadmap or changelog (these reveal development priorities)
- What features they do not mention (notable absences can signal a strategic choice or a product gap)

### 4. Pricing

If pricing is public, document it. Note the tier names, the price points, what each tier includes, and what the upgrade triggers are. Upgrade triggers (usually seat limits, feature locks, or usage caps) reveal what the company believes drives value.

If pricing is not public, note that. Enterprise-negotiated pricing is a signal in itself. It usually means the company is targeting accounts where the sales cycle is long and the contract is large.

Also check for a free trial or freemium tier. Whether or not they offer a free entry point reveals their acquisition strategy and their product confidence.

### 5. Messaging and Tone

Read ten pieces of their content. Read five product page sections carefully. Get a feel for how they write.

Note:

- Do they use technical language or plain language?
- Do they write for the economic buyer or the end user?
- What is their tone? Formal and enterprise? Casual and startup? Authoritative and practitioner-focused?
- What words and phrases appear repeatedly? These are usually intentional.
- What do they never say? Some companies systematically avoid mentioning price. Others avoid mentioning their company size. Omissions can be as telling as inclusions.

### 6. Marketing Channels

Where does the company spend its attention?

Check their blog for publishing frequency and content type. Do they publish consistently or sporadically? Do they write long-form guides or short posts? Do they focus on SEO-optimized content or thought leadership?

Check social media for posting frequency and what kind of content gets engagement.

Check LinkedIn Ads Library for any paid social campaigns. The ads reveal what message they are testing and what audience they are targeting.

Check Google for branded keywords. Are they running Google Ads on their own brand? On competitor keywords? On category terms?

Check their podcast or video presence. Companies that invest in long-form audio or video content are usually building thought leadership in a specific community.

If you have access to SimilarWeb, Semrush, or Ahrefs data, note their estimated traffic, top traffic sources, and top organic keywords.

### 7. Customer Reviews

Review sites are one of the most valuable and underused sources of competitor intelligence. G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, and product-specific review communities give you unfiltered customer language.

On each review site, look at:

- The overall rating and number of reviews (volume and trend)
- The categories where they consistently score high (this is their real strength, not their marketing claim)
- The categories where they consistently score low (this is likely where they lose deals)
- The specific phrases reviewers use to describe problems. These are the exact words your sales team should be using when positioning against this competitor.
- Who the reviewers are (role, company size, industry)
- The "cons" in positive reviews. Even happy customers will tell you what frustrates them.

Read at least 20 to 30 reviews. Look for patterns. The things that appear in multiple reviews are reliable signals. Isolated complaints are not.

### 8. Traffic and Growth Signals

Traffic data gives you a rough sense of scale and momentum.

Use SimilarWeb for monthly visit estimates, traffic sources (direct, organic, referral, paid, social), and geographic distribution.

Use Ahrefs or Semrush to see:

- How many organic keywords they rank for
- Their estimated organic traffic
- Their top-ranking pages (these reveal what content they have invested in)
- Which keywords they pay for

Also look at:

- LinkedIn employee count and recent headcount changes (LinkedIn shows growth trends over time)
- Job postings: which teams are they hiring for? A company that is aggressively hiring SDRs is investing in outbound. One that is hiring growth engineers is investing in product-led growth. One that is hiring for enterprise sales is moving upmarket.
- Funding history on Crunchbase or PitchBook. Funding rounds, amounts, investors, and timing reveal strategic context.

### 9. Strategic Moves and Signals

What has the company done recently that is different from what it was doing 12 months ago?

Sources:

- Press releases and news coverage
- The company blog for product announcements
- LinkedIn posts from the CEO or leadership team
- Podcast appearances by founders or executives
- Conference talks or keynotes
- Partnership announcements
- Recent acquisitions

Strategic signals to watch for:

Moving upmarket: They launch an enterprise tier or hire their first VP of Enterprise Sales. Pricing jumps significantly. They start mentioning compliance certifications they did not mention before.

Moving downmarket: They launch a free tier or reduce prices on lower tiers. They simplify onboarding. They shift from sales-assisted to self-serve.

Entering a new vertical: They publish content targeting a new industry. They hire domain experts from that industry. They announce a partnership with a player in that vertical.

Building a platform: They launch an API, a partner program, or a marketplace. They start publishing developer documentation more prominently.

### 10. Weaknesses and Gaps

This section requires synthesis. After completing the above research, identify:

Where do customers consistently report frustration? If reviews repeatedly mention slow customer support, a confusing interface, or a specific missing feature, that is a structural weakness, not a one-off complaint.

What does the company seem to avoid talking about? If they never mention scalability on their website and their reviews mention performance issues at scale, that is a gap.

What market segments do they not serve? The companies or use cases absent from their case studies and testimonials are often the ones they cannot close or cannot retain.

Where are they losing deals? If you talk to customers who have evaluated both you and this competitor, ask directly what the deciding factor was. Their answers will confirm or contradict what you found in reviews.

## How to Structure a Competitor Profile

A competitor profile document should be usable by someone who has never heard of the company. Format it so it can be read in 10 minutes or referenced quickly during a sales call.

Recommended structure:

Company snapshot: Name, founding year, estimated employee count, funding stage and total raised, headquarters, CEO name.

Positioning statement: A one or two sentence synthesis of how they describe themselves, in their own language.

Target customer: Role, company size, industry. Be specific.

Core product summary: What the product does and what its primary differentiators are, in plain language.

Pricing: Tier names, price points, what the tiers include, and whether a free option exists.

Key strengths: Three to five things they do well, grounded in customer reviews and observable evidence.

Key weaknesses: Three to five consistent pain points or gaps, grounded in the same sources.

Marketing channels: Where they invest attention, what their content strategy looks like, and what their estimated traffic looks like.

Recent strategic moves: The last two to four significant things they did that signal direction.

How we compare: A brief section (two to four paragraphs) that translates the research into positioning guidance. Where do you win against this competitor? Where should you avoid the comparison?

Sources: A list of where the research came from, including the URLs reviewed, the review sites checked, and any tools used.

## Keeping Profiles Current

A competitor profile written today is accurate today. Competitors change their pricing, messaging, product features, and strategic direction regularly. A profile from 18 months ago can actively mislead the people using it.

Set a recurring review cycle. Quarterly is a reasonable minimum for close competitors. Twice per year for secondary ones.

Also update immediately when:

- A competitor raises a significant funding round
- They launch a major new feature or product line
- They change their pricing structure
- They are acquired or make an acquisition
- You start losing deals to them more frequently

The most useful competitive intelligence programs are continuous, not periodic. Subscribe to competitors' blogs. Set Google Alerts on their name. Follow their leadership on LinkedIn. Watch their job postings monthly.

## Turning Research Into Action

Research is only useful if it changes something.

For the sales team: Translate the weaknesses section into specific talk tracks. "When a prospect mentions they're evaluating [Competitor], here is how to position."

For the marketing team: Translate the review language into messaging. If their customers consistently use the phrase "hard to set up," and you know your onboarding is faster, that belongs in your positioning.

For the product team: Translate the gaps section into a feature opportunity map. Where are their customers frustrated with something your product can address?

For the leadership team: Translate the strategic signals section into a directional summary. "Here is where they are headed and what that means for us."

## What to Ask the User Before Starting

When this skill is triggered, ask for:

1. The competitor's URL and company name
2. What the user's company does and who it serves (to make the comparison section relevant)
3. Whether this is a known competitor or a newly identified one
4. The primary audience for the dossier (sales team, product team, leadership, new hire onboarding)
5. What sources are available (access to paid tools like Semrush, Ahrefs, SimilarWeb, or G2 data)

With a URL and your own company context, produce a full structured profile following the format above.
