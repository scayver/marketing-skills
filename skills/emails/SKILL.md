## What This Skill Does

This skill covers lifecycle email: the sequences and automated flows that run throughout a customer's relationship with your product. This is distinct from cold outreach (one-to-one prospecting emails) and from broadcast newsletters (editorial content sent to your whole list).

Lifecycle email is the most consistent revenue-generating email activity in SaaS. It works because it reaches people at the right moment, in response to something they just did, rather than on your schedule.

The central question that drives better lifecycle email: what does the person receiving this email need to believe or do next, and what would stop them?

---

## Part 1: Understanding the Email Lifecycle Framework

Every SaaS customer passes through predictable stages. Your email program should match those stages.

**Stage 1: Subscriber (pre-signup).** Someone gave you their email but has not started a trial or made a purchase. They are in the consideration phase.

**Stage 2: Trial or free user (post-signup, pre-conversion).** They have access to your product but have not paid. This is the highest-leverage email window.

**Stage 3: New paid customer (first 30 to 90 days).** They paid. Now you need to ensure they actually use the product and get results.

**Stage 4: Active customer (ongoing).** They are engaged. Your emails here expand usage, prevent churn, and build loyalty.

**Stage 5: At-risk or churned.** Engagement dropped. They may be about to cancel or already have. Re-engagement and win-back sequences apply here.

Each stage needs a different type of email with a different goal. Most companies invest heavily in Stage 2 and neglect Stages 3 through 5. That imbalance shows up in churn rates.

## Part 2: The Welcome Sequence

The welcome sequence runs for subscribers who join your email list before signing up for a trial or product. Its job is to move them from "I'm interested" to "I'm ready to try this."

A welcome sequence typically runs 5 to 7 emails over 10 to 14 days.

### Email 1: Immediate Welcome (Sent Within Minutes)

This is the most opened email in any sequence. Open rates often exceed 60%. Do not waste it.

Structure:
- Confirm what they signed up for (what they will receive from you).
- Deliver any promised lead magnet or resource immediately.
- Set expectations for what they will receive next.
- Include one clear call to action: usually to read one specific piece of content or to start a trial.

Keep this email short. Under 200 words. One link.

### Email 2: The Problem (Day 2 or 3)

This email addresses the problem your product solves. Write it as though you are describing the reader's situation back to them.

Do not lead with your product. Lead with the pain.

Example opening: "If you're managing social content for more than five clients, you've probably spent a Monday morning rebuilding a spreadsheet someone else deleted."

The second half of this email introduces the concept of a better approach, but does not pitch the product directly. End with a question or an invitation to reply.

### Email 3: The Alternative (Day 4 or 5)

Describe how people typically try to solve the problem: manual processes, stitching together tools, spreadsheets, expensive agencies. Name these alternatives without mocking them.

Then describe the tradeoffs: the alternatives work until they don't, and the failure point is usually when volume or complexity increases.

This email positions your product as the next logical step without saying so directly.

### Email 4: The Case Study (Day 6 or 7)

Share a specific customer story. Name the company (with permission) or describe the situation in detail without naming them. Include before and after specifics.

Weak case study email: "Our customers save time and money."
Strong case study email: "A marketing team at a 30-person fintech company was sending reports to six stakeholders every Friday. It took two people most of Thursday. Now one person reviews and sends by noon."

The specificity is what makes it believable.

### Email 5: The Objection Handler (Day 8 or 9)

Address the most common reasons someone would not sign up at this point. Common objections for SaaS products include:

- "I don't have time to set this up."
- "We tried something like this before and it didn't stick."
- "I'm not sure it will work for our specific situation."
- "The price seems high for what we get."

Write one email that addresses one primary objection in depth. Do not try to handle all objections in one email.

### Email 6: The Soft CTA (Day 10 or 11)

Make a direct invitation to start a trial or take the next step. Keep the tone conversational, not salesy.

A line like "If any of the last week has resonated, the trial takes about ten minutes to set up and the first [specific feature] usually shows value within the first hour" is specific enough to feel honest.

### Email 7: The Last Chance (Day 13 or 14)

Mention that this is the last email of the sequence. Make a final, low-pressure invitation. Offer an alternative: reply with a question, book a demo, or follow on social media.

This email often converts subscribers who have been reading but waiting. The "last email" framing creates a mild incentive to act.

## Part 3: The Onboarding Email Flow

Onboarding emails trigger based on user behavior inside the product. They are the most important emails you will ever write.

The goal: get users to the moment where they understand what the product does for them. This moment is called activation. Every onboarding email serves that goal.

### Define Activation First

Before writing a single onboarding email, define what activation looks like for your product. Activation is not "logged in twice" or "completed the profile." Activation is the moment the user has seen meaningful value.

For a project management tool, activation might be: created a project, invited a team member, and completed a task.
For a reporting tool, activation might be: connected a data source and generated the first report.
For a writing tool, activation might be: produced a draft and clicked "copy."

Write this down. Every onboarding email should push the user toward the activation milestone.

### Onboarding Email Map

Plan your onboarding sequence as a map, not just a linear series.

**Branch based on behavior:** If a user completes activation in the first 24 hours, they should receive a different email than someone who has not opened the product at all.

**Use trigger-based sends instead of time-based sends where possible.** "If no login in 48 hours, send Email 2" is more effective than "send Email 2 to everyone on Day 2."

Standard onboarding map:

**Email 1 (Immediately after signup):** Welcome and single next step. Tell them exactly one thing to do: "Start by connecting your first data source. Here's how." Do not list five things. One thing.

**Email 2 (24 hours after signup, if not activated):** Address the most common stumbling block in your setup process. "Most people get stuck here" emails have very high click-through rates because they feel like help, not marketing.

**Email 3 (48 hours after signup, if not activated):** Show what success looks like. A screenshot of an activated account with a real use case. "This is what [Product] looks like after setup."

**Email 4 (72 hours after signup, if not activated):** Offer a shortcut. A quick-start template, a pre-built example, or an invitation to a setup call. Lower the activation barrier.

**Email 5 (7 days after signup, if not activated):** Check in with a genuine question. "Did something get in the way?" This email often surfaces real blockers (confusion about pricing, a technical issue, a competing priority) that your team can address.

**Activation email (Sent immediately when the user activates):** Congratulate them on completing the first step. Tell them what to do next. This is the moment you have earned the most goodwill. Use it to point them toward the next milestone.

## Part 4: Nurture Sequences for Non-Converting Leads

Not every trial user converts in the first 14 days. A nurture sequence keeps the relationship alive with prospects who showed interest but are not ready yet.

A nurture sequence differs from the welcome sequence. It assumes the person has already experienced the product and needs ongoing reminders of its value or a reason to return.

### Nurture Sequence Structure (8 to 12 Emails Over 60 to 90 Days)

**Weeks 1 to 2:** Educational content tied to the problem your product solves. Not product pitches. Genuinely useful content on the topic your product addresses. A tool for social media scheduling might send an email about the best posting frequency by platform. The email establishes expertise and keeps the brand present.

**Weeks 3 to 4:** Social proof and case studies. Different angles than what appeared in the welcome sequence. Segment by industry or company size if possible.

**Weeks 5 to 6:** Feature spotlight emails. One email per email, one feature. Describe a specific problem the feature solves and who benefits most.

**Weeks 7 to 8:** Soft re-engagement. "We've been sending you content for a while. Are you still interested in [specific outcome]?" Offer to send fewer emails or to answer a specific question.

**Weeks 9 to 12:** Offer-based conversion emails. Limited-time pricing, extended trial, onboarding support offer. These convert the segment that needed a nudge.

## Part 5: Re-Engagement Campaigns

Re-engagement campaigns target subscribers or users who have gone quiet. You define "quiet" by your product: no login in 30 days, no email open in 60 days, or no action in 90 days.

The goal of re-engagement is not to convert everyone. It is to identify who is still reachable and archive everyone else. Keeping disengaged contacts on your list hurts deliverability.

### Re-Engagement Email Structure (3 to 5 Emails Over 2 to 3 Weeks)

**Email 1: The check-in.** Simple, direct. "We noticed you haven't [action] in a while. Is everything okay with your account?" No pressure. Just a genuine check.

**Email 2: The update.** "A lot has changed since you last logged in." Share your two or three most significant product updates or improvements. This works well if you have released new features.

**Email 3: The offer.** A specific incentive to return. Extended access, a free add-on feature, or a discount. Make it concrete and time-limited.

**Email 4: The ultimatum (gently worded).** "We're going to remove you from our list in three days unless you'd like to stay." This email has a high open rate because of the subject line. It filters your list to people who actively choose to remain.

**Email 5: The farewell.** Sent to people who did not re-engage. Confirm their removal. Offer one last way to return. This sometimes triggers a response from people who intended to re-engage and forgot.

After the sequence, archive or tag all non-responders. Stop emailing them.

## Part 6: Trigger-Based Emails

Trigger-based emails fire in response to a specific action or lack of action. They are the highest-performing email type by nearly every metric.

### Common Trigger Email Types

**Trial expiry warning (3 days before, 1 day before):** Tell users their trial expires soon. Include a clear path to upgrade. Include a link to billing. Include the specific features they will lose access to.

**Feature discovery emails:** "You've been using [Feature A] for a few weeks. Have you tried [Feature B] yet? It works well with [Feature A]." Fires 14 days after a user first uses a specific feature.

**Milestone emails:** "You just [reached 100 contacts / sent your 10th report / connected your third integration]. That's significant." Celebrate progress. These emails increase emotional connection with the product.

**Inactivity trigger:** "You haven't [key action] in 7 days. Here's a quick way to get back on track." Fires when a previously active user goes quiet.

**Team invitation prompt:** For collaborative tools, fires after a user has been active solo for 7 days. "Most teams using [product] invite at least two members in the first week. It typically cuts setup time by half."

**Upgrade prompt based on usage limit:** "You've used 80% of your [messages / contacts / projects] this month. Here's what happens at 100% and how to upgrade."

### Setting Up Triggers Correctly

Triggers require your email tool to receive behavioral data from your product. The most common integrations:

- Segment (data layer) connected to your email tool.
- Direct API integration between your product and Intercom, Customer.io, or Klaviyo.
- Zapier or Make automations for simpler products.

The behavioral data you need for triggers:
- Last login date.
- Feature usage events.
- Plan tier.
- Trial start and end date.
- Team size (number of users invited).

Prioritize setting up these data connections before building elaborate sequences. A simple trigger-based flow with accurate data outperforms a complex sequence running on guesses.

## Part 7: Email Cadence Planning

How often you email matters as much as what you email. Too infrequent and people forget you. Too frequent and they unsubscribe.

### Cadence Guidelines by Stage

**Welcome sequence:** Daily or every other day is acceptable. People who just subscribed have high intent and expect to hear from you.

**Onboarding flow:** Trigger-based is better than calendar-based. The right time to send an onboarding email is when the user needs help, not at the same time every day.

**Nurture sequence:** Once per week is the standard for most B2B products. Twice per week if your content is genuinely useful and short. Once every two weeks for enterprise-focused products where contacts are busy.

**Active customer emails:** Once to twice per month for product updates and education. Do not over-email people who are already paying and using the product.

**Re-engagement:** 3 to 5 emails over 2 to 3 weeks. Front-loaded frequency (emails 1 and 2 close together, then space out).

### Total Email Load Audit

Map out all the emails a user could receive from you in their first 30 days. Count them. If the total exceeds 15, you are probably over-emailing. Look for overlap and redundancy. Merge or cut.

A simple test: if you printed all your onboarding emails and read them back to back, would they feel like a coherent conversation or like a collection of disconnected pitches?

## Part 8: Subject Line Writing

Subject lines determine whether your email gets opened. Everything else is secondary.

### Subject Line Principles

**Specificity beats cleverness.** "Your trial ends Thursday" beats "Don't miss out." "3 people on your team haven't logged in yet" beats "Drive adoption with your team."

**Ask a question when you want to create curiosity.** "Are you making this reporting mistake?" works better than "Avoid reporting mistakes."

**Personalization increases open rates.** Using the subscriber's first name or company name in the subject line adds a lift, especially for cold or re-engagement emails. Do not overuse it.

**Short subject lines work on mobile.** Under 50 characters is visible on most mobile screens without truncation.

**Preview text is part of the subject line.** Most email clients show 40 to 100 characters of preview text next to the subject line. Write it as a continuation of the subject. "Your trial ends Thursday" / "Here's what you'll lose access to (and how to keep it)" is one unit.

### Subject Line Testing

Test one variable at a time: subject line only, not subject line plus email body in the same test.

Run A/B tests with a 20% / 20% split. Wait four hours for the winner. Then send the winner to the remaining 60%.

Track open rates by subject line pattern over time. You will find that your audience responds to certain types consistently (questions, urgency, specificity, curiosity) and responds weakly to others. Build a library of what works.

## Part 9: Email Body Structure

People scan emails before they read them. Structure your email to reward scanning.

### Body Structure Principles

**First sentence matters most.** Email preview shows the first line of the body. Write the first sentence as a standalone statement that earns the scroll.

**One email, one message.** If you have two things to say, write two emails.

**Short paragraphs.** Two to three sentences maximum. Single sentences are fine.

**One CTA per email.** If you include multiple links, you dilute the action you want. Choose the most important action and make it the only link. (Or include it twice: once in the body, once at the end.)

**Plain text outperforms HTML in many contexts.** For nurture and relationship emails, plain text reads like a message from a person. For product update emails, HTML with your brand design is appropriate.

**Signature.** Sign from a specific person, not "The [Company] Team." People reply more often when they know a name.

## Part 10: Measuring Email Sequence Performance

Track these metrics for every sequence:

**Open rate:** Industry benchmark for SaaS is 30 to 45% for onboarding, 20 to 30% for nurture. Subject lines drive this metric.

**Click-through rate (CTR):** The percentage of openers who click. 3 to 8% is typical. Higher for highly targeted trigger emails.

**Conversion rate:** The percentage of people who complete the desired action. Define this per email (activate, upgrade, book a demo, re-engage).

**Unsubscribe rate:** Over 0.5% per email is a signal that something is wrong: too frequent, wrong audience, or irrelevant content.

**Reply rate:** For plain-text conversational emails, replies indicate resonance. A 2 to 5% reply rate on a welcome or check-in email is healthy.

**Revenue attribution:** Track which emails appear in the conversion path for new customers. Most email tools and CRMs allow you to see this. If nurture email 6 consistently appears in the path for converting trials, it is more valuable than it might look on open rate alone.

### Email Sequence Audit Process

Every 90 days, audit your sequences:

- Check open rates by email number. Look for drop-offs. A steep open rate decline at Email 4 means emails 1 to 3 are not setting up the value of email 4.
- Read each email in the sequence as a reader, not as a marketer. Does it feel coherent? Does it assume too much knowledge?
- Compare the sequence to your most recent customer research. Do the pain points and language still match what you are hearing from customers?

Update sequences when they go stale. A sequence written 18 months ago with outdated features or old case studies is actively hurting your conversion rate.
