## What This Skill Does

This skill builds community marketing strategies from scratch or improves existing ones. It applies to any product or brand that wants to grow through its users rather than around them.

The outputs include community strategy documents, platform recommendations, engagement frameworks, ambassador program structures, and content calendars built around community participation.

Before writing anything, gather:

- What the product does and who uses it
- Whether a community exists already or is being built from scratch
- Which platforms are under consideration (Discord, Slack, Circle, Reddit, forum, LinkedIn group)
- What the business goal is (retention, acquisition, product feedback, content creation, support deflection)
- The current size and engagement rate of any existing community

## Why Community Marketing Is Different

Most marketing channels involve a brand talking to an audience. Community marketing involves a brand building conditions where the audience talks to each other.

That distinction matters because peer conversation has a credibility premium that brand content does not. When a customer tells another potential customer that a product solved their problem, that carries more weight than any ad or landing page.

The question is not whether community can drive growth. Duolingo, Figma, Notion, and Webflow have all built significant portions of their retention and acquisition through community. The question is what kind of community your product can genuinely support, and what infrastructure it needs to function.

## The Community-Led Growth Flywheel

Community-led growth is not about hosting a Discord and hoping users show up. It is a flywheel with specific, identifiable stages.

Stage 1: Engagement. New members join and find value in existing conversations, resources, or connections. They get a question answered. They see someone's use case and realize the product can do something they did not know about. They feel less alone with a problem they thought was unique to them.

Stage 2: Contribution. Some percentage of engaged members start contributing. They answer someone else's question. They share a workflow they built. They write a post about a problem they solved. Contribution is the hinge point. A community with only consumers and no contributors is not a community; it is an audience.

Stage 3: Advocacy. Contributors who find value in the community start telling others about it. They recommend the product in conversations on other platforms. They share community content. They bring their colleagues in. This is the acquisition component of community-led growth.

Stage 4: Amplification. The community becomes a content engine. User-generated content, case studies, tutorials, and discussions exist independent of the brand. Search engines index them. Social platforms surface them. New potential customers find the product through community content before they ever see an ad.

The flywheel compounds over time. That is its strength, and also the reason it takes longer to build than a paid channel.

## Choosing the Right Platform

Platform choice depends on your audience's behavior, not your preference.

Discord works best for communities centered on real-time conversation, especially in developer, gaming, crypto, and creator markets. The channel structure encourages discovery. The voice and video features add a dimension that text-only platforms lack. Discord's weakness is searchability: it is hard for new members to find answers to questions that were already answered six months ago.

Slack works better for professional communities where real-time but structured conversation is the norm. Many developer communities and B2B SaaS companies use Slack because their users are already in Slack all day. The limitation is cost at scale and the fact that free Slack workspaces lose message history after 90 days, which hurts the searchable knowledge base.

Circle is a purpose-built community platform that works well for creator communities, cohort-based courses, and brands that want more control over the experience than Discord or Slack provides. It has better content organization and stronger member directory features.

Reddit and existing subreddits are different from owned communities. You do not own a subreddit, and you should not try to run one like a brand page. The playbook for subreddits is participation, not broadcasting. Brands that do it well contribute value in their niche's subreddit over time, earn credibility, and let that credibility do the marketing work.

Forums (built on Discourse or similar) work well for communities where the primary value is searchable answers over time. Technical communities, hobbyist communities, and products with complex use cases benefit from forum structures.

The right answer is usually the platform where your users already spend time. Do not ask them to adopt a new platform and a new product simultaneously.

## Building a Community Strategy Document

A community strategy document answers six questions.

Who is the community for? This is not "our customers." It is a specific person with specific problems, goals, and motivations for joining. The more specific the better. "Freelance designers who use Figma daily and want to level up their workflow" is more useful than "designers."

What value does the community deliver to members? People join communities to learn, to connect, to get help, or to get recognition. Your community should be clear about which of these it primarily delivers. Communities that try to be all things to all members often end up being none of them.

What is the primary goal for the business? Retention, acquisition, support deflection, product feedback, or content generation. These are not mutually exclusive, but prioritizing one shapes how you measure and manage the community.

What does success look like in 90 days, 6 months, and 12 months? Define metrics before you launch. Active members per week, content posts per week, member-initiated conversations, Net Promoter Score within the community, and community-sourced product improvements are all trackable.

What does the moderation structure look like? Who approves new members? Who answers questions the moderators do not know? How do you handle conflict? What gets removed and what gets kept?

How does the community connect back to the product? The best communities have a tight relationship between community activity and product value. Members who are active in the community tend to use the product more. The community surfaces product feedback that improves the product, which gives members more to talk about.

## Launching a Community

Cold start is the hardest part of community building. An empty room is uninviting. The first 50 members need to feel like they joined something, not something that might someday become something.

A practical launch sequence:

First, build with 20 to 50 founding members before any public launch. These are your most engaged customers, people who have emailed you feedback, your beta users, your advocates. Invite them personally. Tell them you want their input on shaping the community. Give them a reason to feel invested.

Second, seed the community with content before others arrive. Populate the channels or forums with questions, use cases, and discussions that represent the conversations you want the community to have. New members should land in a space that feels active.

Third, make the first experience frictionless. Most community platforms let you set up an onboarding flow. Use it. Ask members one question when they join ("what brought you here?" or "what's the one thing you're trying to solve?") and have someone respond to every answer in the first 30 days.

Fourth, create early wins for new members. Give them a reason to participate immediately. A quick-start challenge, a "share your setup" thread, or a "introduce yourself" channel that people actually engage with. Early participation predicts long-term retention.

## Engagement Tactics That Work

Engagement is not about posting more content. It is about creating conditions where members choose to participate.

Weekly rituals give members a predictable reason to show up. Examples: a Monday "what are you building this week?" thread, a Friday wins post, a monthly AMA with a practitioner in the field. Rituals reduce the activation energy required to engage.

Direct recognition increases contribution. Call out members who share good content. Feature user-generated work in a weekly digest. Give a "community pick" badge to posts that generate useful conversation. People contribute more when contribution is acknowledged.

Expert access is a high-value lever. AMAs (ask me anything) with practitioners, founders, or relevant experts give members something they cannot get elsewhere. The community becomes the place where access happens.

Product-adjacent events bring the community and the product together. Office hours where the product team answers questions live. Beta previews shared with community members before the general public. Feature voting where community members have real influence. These events create a sense of membership that a passive audience never has.

Peer spotlights work better than brand spotlights. Instead of writing a case study about how a customer uses your product, ask the customer to share how they use it in their own words in the community. The community learns something, the member gets recognition, and the content is more credible than anything the brand could write.

## Ambassador Programs

An ambassador program formalizes the relationship with your most engaged advocates. Done well, it accelerates community growth and extends your reach into new audiences. Done poorly, it feels transactional and alienates the people you most want to keep.

The baseline criteria for ambassadors:

They are active in the community without being paid to be. If someone only shows up when there is something in it for them, they are not an ambassador. They are a contractor.

They have an audience or influence in the spaces where your potential customers spend time. This does not mean they need 100,000 followers. It might mean they are respected in a specific Slack group, or that their blog is read by the right people, or that they speak at local events in your industry.

They use the product regularly and have specific knowledge to share.

What to give ambassadors:

Early access to features before the public. This is valuable to them and costs you almost nothing.

A direct line to the product team. The ability to give feedback and know it will be heard.

Exclusive content or events. A private Slack channel with the team. A quarterly call with the CEO or CPO.

Public recognition. A page on your website with their profiles. An "official ambassador" badge in the community.

Financial compensation is optional and depends on what you are asking ambassadors to do. If you expect them to create content, speak at events, or run local meetups, some form of compensation is appropriate. If the program is primarily about recognition and access, financial compensation is less necessary and can actually make the relationship feel less authentic.

What to ask ambassadors to do:

Be specific. "Help us grow the community" is not a job description. "Write one post per month sharing how you use [feature]" is. "Host one office hours session per quarter" is. "Respond to at least five community questions per week" is.

Set expectations in writing. Not a legal contract necessarily, but a clear document that outlines what the program involves, what you provide, and what you expect. Ambiguity is what turns good advocates into disengaged ones.

## Turning Customers Into Evangelists

An evangelist is different from an ambassador. An ambassador has a formal relationship with you. An evangelist simply talks about your product because they believe in it.

The conditions that produce evangelists:

The product solves a problem the user genuinely cares about. You cannot manufacture this. But you can find the customers for whom the product is most valuable and invest in them.

The user feels seen by the brand. This comes from responding to their feedback, acknowledging their contribution, and treating them as a partner rather than a revenue unit.

The user has something to gain from sharing. Sometimes this is status (being seen as someone who finds good tools). Sometimes it is reciprocity (they want to help others the way someone helped them). Sometimes it is genuine enthusiasm. All three are valid.

Practical actions to accelerate evangelism:

Follow up with new customers 30 days after they sign up. Ask what is working. Acknowledge what they are doing well. People remember who noticed.

Create shareable moments inside the product. "Powered by X" in exported files. Social sharing built into milestones. A public profile or portfolio that lives at a URL they can send to others.

Make it easy to share. Remove friction from the act of recommending you. A referral link, a pre-written tweet, a one-pager they can forward to a colleague.

## Subreddit and Forum Strategy

You do not own subreddits. You participate in them. The approach is completely different from owned community management.

First, identify the subreddits where your audience actually discusses the problems your product solves. Use Reddit's search and also tools like GummySearch to find communities where relevant conversations happen.

Second, spend 60 to 90 days reading before posting anything. Learn the culture, the recurring questions, the respected voices, the things that get upvoted, and the things that get downvoted or removed.

Third, contribute value before you mention your product. Answer questions genuinely. Share resources that help people even if those resources have nothing to do with your company. Earn the reputation of someone who contributes.

Fourth, when you do mention your product, be transparent. Disclose that you work there. Frame it as one option among others. Let the community decide what they think. Heavy-handed promotion on Reddit gets flagged and destroys the credibility you built.

The ROI on subreddit strategy is long-term. It takes time. But a brand that is genuinely helpful and respected in a niche subreddit builds trust that no ad can replicate.

## Measuring Community Performance

Vanity metrics to avoid:

Total members is nearly meaningless without context. A community of 10,000 dormant members is less valuable than a community of 500 active ones.

Metrics that matter:

Monthly active members (members who post, comment, or react at least once per month). This is your true community size.

Post-to-member ratio (number of posts per active member per week). Healthy communities have multiple posts per member. Communities in decay have fewer.

Response rate and response time (what percentage of questions get answered, and how quickly). This measures whether the community delivers on its core promise.

Member retention (what percentage of members who joined three months ago are still active today). Community churn is real and often undertracked.

Community-attributed pipeline or revenue, if your product involves a purchase decision. This requires tagging new trials or purchases that came through community referral links or community content.

Net Promoter Score within the community. If your community members would not recommend the community to a colleague, the community is not delivering enough value.

## What to Ask the User Before Writing

When this skill is triggered, ask for:

1. The product and the target user persona
2. The business goal for the community (retention, acquisition, support deflection, etc.)
3. Existing community platforms, if any, and current member counts and engagement rates
