## What This Skill Does

This skill helps you make pricing strategy decisions for SaaS and software products. That includes choosing a pricing model, structuring your tiers, identifying your value metric, researching willingness to pay, designing your pricing page, and handling price increases.

Pricing is the most underinvested area in most SaaS companies. Most founders set prices based on what competitors charge, adjust them rarely, and leave significant revenue on the table. This skill treats pricing as a repeatable discipline with specific research methods, not a gut-feel decision made once at launch.

The question worth sitting with before any pricing work: what is the unit of value your customers receive from your product? Not the feature they use, but the business outcome they get. Your pricing should scale with that.

## Pricing Model Selection

Your pricing model determines how you charge customers. Different models fit different products. Choosing the wrong model makes pricing feel wrong to customers even if the number itself is correct.

### Per Seat (User-Based) Pricing

Per seat pricing charges customers based on the number of users in their account. Every additional user costs more.

When per seat pricing works well: collaboration tools where every team member uses the product directly. Slack, Notion, Linear, Figma. The value scales with the number of users because more users means more collaboration value.

When per seat pricing creates problems: tools that are primarily used by one or two power users but deliver value across a whole organization. A CMO who uses your analytics tool to pull reports for their whole team should not have to add 20 user seats for people who never log in.

Per seat pricing also creates a perverse incentive: customers minimize the number of seats they purchase, which means they under-adopt the product. If customers are constantly trying to share one login across their team, your pricing model is working against your own retention.

### Usage-Based Pricing

Usage-based pricing charges customers based on how much they use the product. This could be API calls, messages sent, emails processed, documents generated, or any other measurable consumption unit.

When usage-based pricing works well: products where consumption scales naturally with value. A transactional email service charges per email sent because a customer who sends 10 million emails gets more value than one who sends 10,000. Stripe charges a percentage of revenue processed because a business that processes $10M gets more value than one processing $10,000.

When usage-based pricing creates problems: when the usage metric does not align with the value the customer receives. If customers feel uncertain about their bill, they hesitate to use the product. This friction reduces adoption and retention.

Usage-based pricing also makes revenue unpredictable. Your MRR fluctuates with customer usage patterns. This is acceptable at scale but can be challenging early.

The hybrid solution: usage-based pricing within a seat or subscription structure. A flat monthly subscription that includes a usage allowance, with overage charges for usage above the included amount. This gives customers budget predictability while still capturing value from high-usage accounts.

### Flat Rate Pricing

Flat rate pricing is one price for all customers, regardless of seats or usage. Everyone pays the same amount.

When flat rate pricing works: when your product has a natural single-use case and a relatively homogeneous customer base. Early-stage products with a narrow ICP sometimes start with flat rate pricing for simplicity.

When flat rate pricing fails: when your customers vary significantly in the value they get from your product. A small team using your tool for 5 projects per month and a large team using it for 500 projects per month pay the same. You are leaving revenue on the table from the high-value customer and possibly overcharging the low-value customer.

Flat rate pricing also makes expansion revenue nearly impossible. Your customers cannot grow their payment with you as they grow their usage.

### Freemium

Freemium offers a permanently free tier of the product alongside paid plans. The free tier is not a trial. It does not expire. Users can use it indefinitely.

When freemium works: when the free tier creates genuine value that drives word-of-mouth and product adoption, and when a meaningful percentage of free users eventually need the paid tier to get full value. Figma, Notion, Slack, and Calendly all built significant businesses on freemium.

When freemium fails: when the free tier is so limited that users cannot experience meaningful value, which means they never advocate for the product. Or when the free tier is so generous that users never need to pay, which means you are providing value without capturing it.

The freemium calculation: your free tier creates costs (infrastructure, support, onboarding). Those costs must be offset by the percentage of free users who convert to paid and by the advocacy and word-of-mouth the free tier generates. If your conversion rate from free to paid is 2-5% and your cost to serve free users is manageable, freemium can work. If your conversion rate is below 1%, your free tier is too generous.

## Value Metric Identification

Your value metric is the unit that best correlates with the value your customers get from your product. It is what your pricing should scale with.

The right value metric has three properties. First, it scales with customer success: as the customer gets more value, the metric increases. Second, it is understandable: customers should be able to predict and control their own costs. Third, it is easy to measure: you can track it accurately and customers can verify it.

Common value metrics by product type:

A project management tool: active projects, team members, or tasks completed.
An email marketing tool: contacts stored or emails sent.
A reporting tool: reports generated, data sources connected, or seats.
A CRM: contacts in the system, deals tracked, or seats.
An API: API calls, data transferred, or operations executed.

How to identify your value metric:

Talk to your highest-value customers. What would they say they are getting from your product? In what unit do they measure that value? If they say "we save 10 hours per week per analyst," the unit is analyst hours. If they say "we closed 40% more deals since using this," the unit is deals or revenue.

Look at your retention data. What distinguishes customers who stay from customers who churn? Often, customers who use more of a specific feature retain better. That feature usage may point to your value metric.

Ask customers directly: "If you used this product 10x more than you do now, what would be 10x better in your business?" The answer tells you what scales with usage.

## Tier Structure Design

Most SaaS products use three tiers. Three tiers is not arbitrary. It is the structure that best applies the decoy effect (covered in the marketing-psychology skill) while giving buyers enough choice to find a natural fit.

The principle of good tier design: each tier should have a natural customer profile. If you look at your existing customers, you should be able to assign roughly 15-25% to Tier 1, 50-60% to Tier 2, and 20-30% to Tier 3. If all your customers cluster in one tier, your tier structure does not segment the market correctly.

How to design your three tiers:

Start with your target customer in Tier 2. Tier 2 is your primary revenue driver. Design it around the customer you most want to serve and the price you need them to pay.

Design Tier 1 for customers who cannot yet afford Tier 2 or have smaller needs. Do not over-restrict Tier 1. Users who outgrow Tier 1 should feel a clear pull toward Tier 2, not feel that the product is no longer for them.

Design Tier 3 for customers with enterprise needs: more seats, more usage, more security or compliance features, dedicated support. Tier 3 does not need to have a public price. It can be "Contact us."

What to put in each tier:

The features that differentiate your tiers should be the features that larger or more sophisticated customers need, not features that every customer needs to use the product. Gating basic functionality in Tier 1 creates resentment. Gating advanced reporting, admin controls, and integrations in Tier 2 and 3 is reasonable.

How to name your tiers: names should signal the customer profile, not be abstract labels. "Starter," "Pro," and "Business" are functional. "Team," "Growth," and "Scale" communicate where a company is in its journey. "Basic," "Plus," and "Premium" position the lower tiers as lesser products. Avoid that.

## Van Westendorp Pricing Research

The Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter is a survey-based research method that identifies an acceptable price range for your product by asking four questions.

The four questions:

1. At what price would you consider this product so cheap that you would question its quality?
2. At what price would you consider this product to be a bargain (good value for money)?
3. At what price would you consider this product to be getting expensive, though you would still consider buying it?
4. At what price would you consider this product so expensive that you would not consider buying it?

You plot the cumulative frequency curves for each question. The intersection of certain curves gives you:

The acceptable price range: where most respondents would consider the product acceptable quality and not too expensive.

The optimal price point: where resistance to both "too cheap" and "too expensive" is minimized.

Who to survey: your ICP. Not your existing customers (they are already anchored to your current price). Potential customers who match your target profile. Minimum 50-100 responses for meaningful results.

The limitation of Van Westendorp: it tells you what people say they would pay, not what they would actually pay. Stated willingness to pay consistently overestimates actual willingness to pay. Use Van Westendorp to identify a range, then test actual prices to validate.

## Willingness to Pay Research

Beyond Van Westendorp, there are additional approaches to understanding what customers will actually pay.

Conjoint analysis: a research method where you show respondents pairs of product configurations with different prices and ask which they prefer. The pattern of choices reveals how much each feature is worth and where price sensitivity kicks in. More rigorous than Van Westendorp but requires specialized survey design.

Actual price testing: show different pricing to different visitor segments and measure actual conversion. This is the most accurate form of willingness-to-pay research because it measures behavior, not stated intent. It requires enough traffic to reach statistical significance and clear ethical guidelines about whether you are showing different prices to existing customers.

Sales conversations: your sales team hears about price sensitivity every day. Systematically capture where deals are lost due to price, what pricing objections come up most frequently, and what competitors customers say they considered. This is free research you are probably not collecting consistently.

Customer interviews: ask current customers directly. "How did you feel about our pricing when you signed up?" and "Is there a pricing structure that would feel more natural for the way your team uses this product?" These conversations reveal both price sensitivity and model fit.

## Price Increase Strategy

Price increases are one of the highest-leverage revenue moves available to most SaaS companies. Most founders raise prices too rarely and too timidly.

When to consider a price increase:

You have not raised prices in more than 18 months.

Your customer success team reports that customers frequently say "I can't believe how much value we get for what we pay."

Your NPS is high and churn is low.

New customers are converting at a rate that exceeds your capacity to onboard them.

Your product has materially improved since your last pricing update.

How to structure a price increase:

Grandfather existing customers for a defined period. "Your current price is locked through December 31. After that, you'll move to our new pricing." This shows respect for existing customer relationships and gives people time to plan.

Communicate the reason. Not just "we're raising prices" but "we've added [X features] and our support team has grown by 50% to [specific improvement]. Our pricing needed to reflect the product you're using today."

Give adequate notice. 60-90 days for annual customers. 30 days for monthly customers.

Offer an upgrade incentive to soften the transition. "Lock in your current rate by upgrading to annual billing before the price change."

How much to raise prices: most SaaS companies that have not raised prices in 18+ months can raise 20-30% with minimal churn impact. The emotional weight of a price increase is felt by the team more than by customers. Data from companies like Basecamp, Baremetrics, and others consistently shows churn spikes of 0-5% from price increases, which are quickly offset by the revenue gain from retained customers paying more.

## Annual vs. Monthly Pricing Psychology

Annual and monthly pricing are not just billing options. They are different psychological propositions.

Monthly pricing: a low-commitment option. The customer can leave any time. This lowers the barrier to entry but also lowers the barrier to churning. Monthly customers churn at higher rates than annual customers.

Annual pricing: a higher-commitment option. The customer locks in. This reduces churn dramatically (annual customers cannot churn mid-year on an annual plan). It also provides you with cash upfront.

The financial case for pushing annual: annual customers typically have 50-80% lower churn than monthly customers. Even if annual customers pay slightly less per month (because you offer a discount), the lifetime value is often 2-3x higher than the equivalent monthly customer.

How to increase annual plan uptake:

Make annual the default option on your pricing page. Show it first. Highlight it as "most popular" or "recommended." Users anchor to whatever they see first.

Express the annual price as a monthly equivalent. "$24/month, billed annually" makes the annual option feel like a monthly decision.

Show the dollar savings clearly. "$49/month" versus "$39/month billed annually (save $120/year)" is more persuasive than the percentage.

Offer annual as the primary option in upgrade flows. When a user hits a paywall or decides to upgrade, offer annual first with the monthly option secondary.

Discount meaningfully: 15-20% is the minimum discount that meaningfully changes behavior. A 5% discount for annual rarely moves people.

## Pricing Page Design Principles

Your pricing page has one job: help the right customer identify the right plan and convert. Every design decision should serve that goal.

Annual billing toggle: put it at the top. Highlight annual as the default. Show savings clearly.

Tier ordering: show tiers from left to right, lowest to highest price. Western audiences scan left to right, so the first option they see feels like the "default" starting point. This works in your favor if you want customers to upgrade: they start at the left and evaluate each tier against the next.

Visual hierarchy within each tier: plan name, price, CTA, key features. That is the order. Do not bury the price below a long feature list.

Feature comparison: use checkmarks for included features and blank cells (not X marks) for excluded features. X marks feel punitive. Blank cells are neutral.

Most popular badge: add a visual indicator to the tier you most want customers to choose. It provides social proof ("other customers chose this") and reduces decision paralysis.

FAQ section: include a short FAQ that addresses the three to five most common pre-purchase questions. Pricing questions that go unanswered become objections that kill conversions.

Clear upgrade and downgrade paths: tell customers how to upgrade, downgrade, or cancel. Opacity around cancellation creates distrust. Transparency creates confidence.

One clear CTA per tier: one button per plan. Not "Start free trial / Contact sales / See demo." One action.

## Common Pricing Mistakes

Pricing based on cost: your cost to build and operate the product has almost no bearing on what customers will pay. Price based on value delivered, not cost incurred.

Copying competitors without context: competitor pricing is useful data, not a template. Your cost structure, customer profile, and product positioning may all justify a different price than what competitors charge.

Under-pricing to win on cost: competing on price in SaaS is almost always a losing strategy. You attract price-sensitive customers who churn when a cheaper option appears, and you leave revenue on the table from customers who would have paid more.

Raising prices reactively: price increases driven by financial pressure (rather than value delivery) are the most poorly communicated and most likely to cause churn. Plan price increases when the product is strongest, not when finances are weakest.

Feature-stuffing tiers: adding features to a tier does not justify a price increase unless those features deliver meaningful additional value. More features that customers do not use are not more valuable.

Ignoring packaging: which features go in which tier is as important as the price of each tier. Many companies have the right prices but the wrong feature allocation, which sends the wrong customers to the wrong tier.

Not testing: pricing can and should be tested. Cohort-based price testing (showing different prices to different groups of new visitors) is standard practice. If you have not tested your prices in the last 12 months, you are flying blind.
