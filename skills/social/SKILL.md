# Social Media Content

## Mandatory Content Standards

- Write between 1,500 and 10,000 words per skill output.
- Write in a way that sounds like a knowledgeable human wrote it. No robotic or templated phrasing.
- Use short sentences. One idea per sentence. One focus per paragraph.
- Use active voice. Never passive constructions.
- Address the reader directly using "you" and "your."
- Use bullet points only when they genuinely improve readability.
- No em dashes. Replace with commas, parentheses, semicolons, or new sentences.
- End every sentence with a period.
- No hashtags, emojis, or asterisks.
- No filler phrases like "in conclusion" or "in summary."
- No warnings or disclaimers.
- No AI cliches: no "game-changer," "unlock," "leverage," "dive into," "cutting-edge," "transformative."
- Use specific examples, data points, and scenarios.
- Pose at least one thought-provoking question.
- Keep paragraphs short. Headers scannable. Structure mobile-friendly.
- Every section connects to a practical next step.

## Platform Selection and Priorities

Not every platform is worth your time. The right platforms depend on your audience, your content format strengths, and your capacity. Spreading effort across five platforms mediocrely produces less than concentrating on two platforms well.

The framework for platform selection is simple. Where does your target audience actually spend time on social? Not where they have accounts. Where they actively scroll and engage? A B2B buyer might have a TikTok account but makes no purchase decisions based on what they see there. That same buyer might spend 20 minutes per day on LinkedIn consuming industry content and engaging with peers.

Here is how to think about each major platform:

**LinkedIn** is the primary platform for B2B audiences. Anyone selling to business buyers, building professional credibility, or trying to reach decision-makers in specific industries should treat LinkedIn as the top priority. The organic reach on LinkedIn is unusually strong compared to most platforms, particularly for content that generates comments.

**Twitter/X** is best for audiences who consume news, technology commentary, and real-time conversation. It works well for developers, journalists, investors, and anyone in a fast-moving industry where people follow what is happening daily. The platform has become harder to grow organically since 2022, but it still has concentrated professional audiences in specific niches.

**Instagram** is a visual platform that works for consumer products, lifestyle brands, design-forward businesses, and B2C audiences. Instagram Reels have significantly better organic reach than static posts.

**TikTok** has the highest organic reach potential of any platform in 2024 and 2025. Short-form video performs across consumer and B2B contexts. Many B2B brands are building audiences there by focusing on educational, personality-driven content rather than promotional content.

**Facebook** is most relevant for consumer audiences above age 35, local businesses, and communities organized around interest groups. Facebook Groups remain active for specific niche communities.

Choose one or two platforms where your audience is genuinely present. Build real presence there before expanding to additional platforms.

## LinkedIn Post Structure

LinkedIn posts are text-first. The algorithm rewards content that generates comments because comments signal genuine engagement rather than passive consumption.

A strong LinkedIn post has five components:

**The hook.** The first one to two lines are the most important. LinkedIn shows these lines before the "see more" cutoff. If the hook does not create enough curiosity or value to make a reader click "see more," the post fails regardless of how good the rest is.

Hooks that work:
- A counterintuitive statement. "Most marketing advice about LinkedIn is wrong."
- A specific, surprising number. "I grew from 2,000 to 18,000 followers in 8 months by posting about one topic."
- A direct question that creates tension. "Why do companies with the best products often lose to companies with worse ones?"
- An admission or vulnerable observation. "I spent three years avoiding LinkedIn because I was afraid of being wrong in public."

Hooks that do not work:
- "I'm excited to announce..." (promotional and generic).
- "In today's fast-paced world..." (cliche).
- Long preamble before the actual point.

**The body.** The body delivers what the hook promised. Write in short paragraphs of one to three sentences each. Add a line break between every paragraph. Long blocks of text are skipped.

The body can take several formats: a list of lessons, a short story with a point, a before-and-after structure, a question followed by a direct answer, or a framework or mental model explained step by step.

**The evidence.** Concrete specifics make posts credible. Replace vague claims with numbers, named examples, and specific situations. "Our team increased response rates" is forgettable. "Our team went from a 2 percent to a 9 percent reply rate on cold outreach by changing one thing" is specific enough to be believed and useful enough to share.

**The point.** Every post needs a central point. If you cannot state in one sentence what the reader should think, feel, or do differently after reading the post, the post needs revision.

**The invitation to engage.** End with a question or an invitation to share experience. "What has worked for you?" or "Drop your answer in the comments" prompts responses. Posts with many comments get amplified by the LinkedIn algorithm.

Avoid including links in the main body of LinkedIn posts. LinkedIn's algorithm suppresses posts with external links because they push users off the platform. If you need to share a link, put it in the first comment and reference that in the post body.

## LinkedIn Carousels

Carousels are multi-image posts that users swipe through. They consistently outperform single-image posts for reach and saves because they deliver a richer, more complete experience and keep users engaged longer.

A LinkedIn carousel has a specific structure:

**Slide 1 (the hook).** The first slide functions like a post hook. It must make the reader want to swipe. "7 things I wish I knew before I hired my first sales team" is a carousel hook. "Our latest product update" is not.

**Slides 2 through N (the content).** Each slide delivers one idea, one step, one lesson, or one insight. Keep the text on each slide short enough to read in three to five seconds. Use large font. Use consistent visual design across all slides.

**Last slide (the takeaway and CTA).** Summarize the core lesson in one sentence. Then include a call to action: follow the account for more, save this post to revisit later, or tag someone who should see this.

Keep carousels between 7 and 15 slides. Fewer than 7 does not justify the swipe format. More than 15 loses most readers before the end.

Use Canva, Figma, or Adobe Express to design carousels. Choose a template you can replicate easily. Consistency in visual style builds brand recognition over time.

Topics that work well as carousels: "X mistakes I see in Y," "how to do X in N steps," "the difference between X and Y," frameworks with visual components, and case studies with before-and-after data.

## Twitter/X Thread Writing

A thread is a series of connected posts that tell a story or make an argument across multiple entries. Threads get far more distribution than single posts on X because engagement on later posts in the thread compounds the reach of the entire thread.

Structure a thread this way:

**Tweet 1 (the hook):** One to two sentences that state the core insight or promise. "I analyzed 500 cold emails that got responses. Here's what they all had in common:" or "A lesson about pricing that cost me $80,000 to learn." This tweet must stand alone as compelling. Many users will only see this tweet.

**Tweets 2 through N (the content):** Each tweet delivers one discrete insight, step, or piece of evidence. Write each tweet so it reads clearly in isolation, not only in the context of the full thread. Some readers scroll into threads mid-way.

Number your tweets if the order matters. "2/10" or "Point 1:" at the start of each tweet helps readers navigate.

**Final tweet (the summary and CTA):** Restate the core insight in one sentence. Then add the call to action: follow for more, retweet the first tweet if it was useful, or reply with your experience.

Thread writing tips:

- Write the hook last. Once you know the full content, you can write the most compelling possible opening.
- Cut ruthlessly. If a tweet does not add new information, remove it.
- Use white space. One sentence per tweet often outperforms three sentences in the same tweet.
- Add images, screenshots, or data visuals to key tweets in the thread. They increase engagement and break up the text.

## Short-Form Video Scripting (Reels, TikTok, Shorts)

Short-form video is the highest-reach format on TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube. A well-structured short video can reach people who have never encountered your brand before. That organic discovery potential is rare and worth building.

A short-form video script follows a specific structure:

**The hook (0 to 3 seconds).** The first three seconds determine whether a viewer keeps watching or scrolls. You need a hook that addresses the viewer's specific situation or curiosity immediately.

The three most reliable hook formats:

- The direct statement. "Most people waste their first year of content marketing doing this." The viewer keeps watching to find out if they are doing it.
- The visual hook. Something visually interesting or unexpected appears in the first frame. The viewer keeps watching to understand what they are seeing.
- The question. "Do you know why your LinkedIn posts get no comments?" The viewer who relates to this keeps watching for the answer.

Avoid starting a video with a logo animation, a "hey everyone" greeting, or background music for the first three seconds. These waste the hook window.

**The body (3 to 45 seconds).** Deliver the content promised by the hook. Be specific. Use visual language, real examples, and clear steps. Cut everything that does not serve the viewer's understanding.

For educational videos, structure the body as a numbered list or a simple framework. "There are three reasons your cold emails get ignored. Number one is..." numbered content gives viewers a mental anchor and makes them want to see all three.

For storytelling videos, the body follows a problem-complication-resolution arc. Problem: here is what was happening. Complication: here is what made it harder. Resolution: here is what changed and what I learned.

**The ending (last 3 to 5 seconds).** Tell the viewer what to do next. "Follow for more like this" is a call to action. "Comment the word X if you want me to share the template" is a call to action that also generates engagement that the algorithm rewards.

For TikTok specifically: the first frame matters enormously. Use text overlays on the first frame that restate the hook. Many viewers scroll with sound off and decide whether to unmute based on the visual.

## Content Repurposing Strategy

Creating original content for five platforms from scratch is not sustainable. The sustainable approach is to create one substantial piece of content and then adapt it into multiple formats for multiple platforms.

The repurposing cascade works like this:

**Original source content:** A long-form piece in your primary format. This could be a 2,000-word article, a 45-minute podcast episode, a webinar, or a detailed report.

**Tier one repurposing (same platform, different format):** Pull one insight from the source content and write a LinkedIn post about it. Pull another insight and make it a carousel. Pull a third and write a short thread on X.

**Tier two repurposing (different platform, adapted format):** Take the most compelling section of the article and script it as a 60-second video for TikTok or Reels. Turn the podcast episode's three main points into a carousel deck for LinkedIn.

**Tier three repurposing (derivative formats):** Turn the article into a downloadable checklist. Turn the video into a transcript-based blog post. Turn the report's key data into an infographic.

One substantial piece of source content can produce 15 to 25 individual social posts across platforms if repurposed systematically. Most of the creative work happens once (in the original piece). The adaptation work is significantly faster.

Build a repurposing workflow:

1. Decide on the source format for each major content topic (article, podcast, video).
2. Create a template that maps the source content to each derivative format.
3. Assign repurposing tasks to a writer or editor who works from the source content, not from scratch.
4. Publish the derivatives across a 2 to 4 week window so the original content continues to drive visibility.

## Posting Frequency by Platform

Posting too infrequently builds no momentum. Posting too frequently on platforms where content is quickly buried wastes production effort. Calibrate frequency to the platform's mechanics.

**LinkedIn:** 3 to 5 times per week is optimal for most creators. The LinkedIn algorithm favors accounts that post consistently, and posts typically remain visible in feeds for 24 to 48 hours. Daily posting is sustainable for accounts with a content system in place, but 3 times per week is a realistic floor for growth.

**Twitter/X:** 1 to 3 posts or threads per day. The X feed moves fast. One post per day builds minimal presence. Threads 2 to 3 times per week with additional shorter posts produces meaningful reach.

**Instagram:** 4 to 7 posts per week across feed and Stories. Reels should be at least 2 to 3 per week if growth is the goal. Instagram deprioritizes accounts that post inconsistently.

**TikTok:** 1 to 3 videos per day is recommended by most creators who have studied TikTok's algorithm. The platform rewards high volume and consistency, especially for new accounts. Posting once per week on TikTok is too slow for most growth goals.

**Facebook:** 3 to 5 posts per week for business pages. Facebook Groups benefit from daily activity from the group admin.

Consistency matters more than maximum frequency. An account that posts 3 times per week reliably outperforms an account that posts 20 times one week and then disappears for two weeks.

## Engagement Tactics

Reach gets you in front of people. Engagement is what builds an audience. The two are different, and many accounts confuse optimizing for reach with optimizing for audience building.

Engagement tactics that consistently work:

**Reply to every comment, especially early.** Comments on posts increase their algorithmic distribution. Responding to comments generates more comments. When a post is new, the first 30 to 60 minutes of engagement has an outsized impact on how broadly it gets distributed. Be present when you post.

**Comment on other people's content authentically.** A genuine, thoughtful comment on a post from someone in your audience's feed exposes your name to their network. The comment appears in your own followers' feeds as "Person X commented on this post." High-quality comments build awareness across networks you cannot reach through your own posts alone.

**Ask specific questions, not open-ended ones.** "What do you think?" generates few responses. "What's one thing your company does in the first week of onboarding that you think other teams miss?" generates specific, shareable responses from people who have something concrete to say.

**Tag people you mention.** When a post references a specific person's idea, company, or work, tagging them is appropriate (not promotional tagging, but genuine attribution). Tagged people often engage, which exposes the post to their network.

**Use polls.** LinkedIn and X both support polls. Polls generate high engagement volume because they require minimal effort from the participant. The data from polls also gives you content ideas and insight into your audience's views.

## Building an Audience from Zero

Starting with zero followers is uncomfortable but not complicated. Every account with a large audience started at zero. The specific question is: what produces early traction before the algorithm provides amplification?

The steps that reliably produce early growth:

**Define a specific niche.** Broad audiences grow slowly. Specific audiences grow faster because your content is highly relevant to a defined group of people who will engage and share. "Marketing tips" is a broad niche. "Growth strategies for early-stage B2B SaaS companies with fewer than 50 employees" is specific enough to own.

**Post consistently for 90 days before evaluating.** The first 30 to 60 days of posting almost always produce slow growth. Algorithms take time to understand your content category and your audience. The accounts that quit in this period do not see the inflection that comes from consistent volume. Commit to 90 days of consistent posting before drawing conclusions.

**Engage heavily in the first 6 months.** Comment on other posts in your niche for 20 to 30 minutes per day. This is not scalable indefinitely, but it is the fastest way to build visibility early when your own posts are not yet reaching many people.

**Collaborate with accounts in adjacent niches.** Guest posts, co-created threads, and joint carousels expose your content to another creator's audience. Identify accounts that serve a similar audience but cover adjacent topics and propose a collaboration.

**Optimize for saves and shares, not likes.** Likes are cheap engagement. Saves (on Instagram and LinkedIn) and shares signal that the content was valuable enough to keep or redistribute. Content optimized for saves tends to be practical and reference-worthy: templates, frameworks, step-by-step guides, and summarized research.

The single most important factor in early audience building is content quality, not posting frequency. One post that genuinely helps 50 people and generates 30 comments will build more audience momentum than five mediocre posts that generate passive scrolling. Invest more time per post in the early phase, and increase volume as you learn what resonates.
