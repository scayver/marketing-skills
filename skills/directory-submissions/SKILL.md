## What This Skill Does

This skill helps you build a systematic process for submitting your product to directories. It covers which directories matter, how to write listing copy that converts, how to track submissions over time, and how to prioritize your effort by SEO and traffic value.

Directory submissions are one of the most underestimated early-stage marketing activities. A single listing on a high-authority directory gives you a dofollow backlink, category visibility, and sometimes meaningful trial traffic. The compounding effect across 30 to 50 directories is real.

The question that should guide your prioritization: which directories rank on page one for the searches your ideal customers actually run?

---

## Part 1: Why Directories Still Matter

Directories are not a relic. For SaaS and software products, they serve three distinct functions.

**Backlinks with domain authority.** G2 has a domain rating above 90. Futurepedia, BetaList, and TAAFT (There's an AI for That) have ratings in the 60 to 80 range. A listing on these sites gives you a link from a trusted domain. At early stages, these links matter more than almost any other link-building activity.

**Category search traffic.** Many directories rank for queries like "best project management software for small teams" or "AI writing tools for marketing." If your product appears on those pages, people actively searching for a solution find you.

**Buyer research behavior.** Enterprise and mid-market buyers check G2 and Capterra as part of their buying process. A listing with reviews and a completed profile makes you look credible when a buyer is comparing you to a competitor.

These three functions compound. A directory listing is not a one-time traffic event. It is a permanent asset that continues to generate backlinks and visibility.

## Part 2: The Directory Landscape

Different directories serve different purposes. Understand the category before submitting.

### Launch and Discovery Directories

These sites surface new products. They generate a spike of traffic on launch day and a small ongoing trickle. Their main value is the backlink and the social signal.

**Product Hunt:** The most visible launch platform for SaaS and tech products. A top-5 finish on launch day can drive hundreds or thousands of sign-ups. Domain rating is very high. Submitting is free but preparation is significant. More on this in the launch skill.

**BetaList:** Focused on early-stage and beta products. Good for pre-launch list building. Domain rating is moderate but the audience is specifically early adopters.

**Launching Next:** Smaller but still relevant for early-stage discovery. Free to submit.

**MakerPad (now Bubble) and Indie Hackers:** Less traditional directories, more community listing pages. Still worth a listing.

### AI-Specific Directories

If your product uses AI in any meaningful way, these directories are essential. They are growing in traffic rapidly because the category is growing.

**There's an AI for That (TAAFT):** One of the highest-traffic AI directories. Ranks for hundreds of AI-related queries. Free listing available. Paid "featured" placement available.

**Futurepedia:** High domain authority, strong organic search presence for AI tool queries. Free and paid options.

**AI Top Tools:** A newer directory with growing authority. Free to submit.

**Toolify:** Large catalog of AI tools. Worth listing for backlink and category visibility.

**AI Directory (aidirectory.org):** Growing quickly in the AI agent and automation category.

**FlowGPT:** Primarily for prompts, but also lists AI tools. Worth listing if your product relates to prompts or AI workflows.

**MCP Directories (emerging):** As the MCP (Model Context Protocol) ecosystem grows, directories specific to MCP servers and agents are appearing. Track and submit to these early. Being listed first in a new category carries long-term SEO value.

**Agent-specific directories:** Sites like AgentDirectory.ai and similar new platforms targeting AI agent tools. Submit early even if traffic is low. These will matter in 12 to 24 months.

### SaaS and Software Directories

**AlternativeTo:** Lists alternatives to popular software. Extremely high domain authority. People searching for alternatives to your competitors will find you here. Critical if you compete with established tools.

**SaaSHub:** A large, well-indexed SaaS directory. Good domain authority. Worth submitting for both the backlink and the "alternatives" pages.

**GetApp:** Owned by Gartner (same parent as Capterra and G2). High authority. Business buyers use it.

**Software Advice:** Also Gartner-owned. Similar to Capterra in audience.

**SourceForge:** Very high domain authority. Primarily developer and open-source tools, but accepts commercial software. Worth listing if relevant.

**Slant.co:** Community-driven comparisons. Users recommend products in categories. Less active now but still indexed.

### Review Platforms

These are different from discovery directories. Review platforms are where buyers go to validate decisions. They carry high buyer intent.

**G2:** The most important review platform for B2B SaaS. Enterprise buyers check G2. A product page with zero reviews looks abandoned. A page with 10 genuine reviews looks legitimate. G2 also ranks for many "[category] software" searches.

**Capterra:** Heavy in SMB and mid-market. Owned by Gartner. Very high domain authority. Capterra reviews also feed into GetApp and Software Advice.

**Trustpilot:** Better for B2C or prosumer software. High domain authority. Shows up in brand searches.

**G2 and Capterra both require you to claim your listing before you can manage it.** Search for your product first. If it exists, claim it. If it does not, create it.

## Part 3: Writing Directory Listing Copy

Every directory listing has the same core components: headline or product name, tagline, short description, long description, category tags, and screenshots or a demo video.

Most founders write generic descriptions. That is the opportunity. Write specific copy that speaks to the problem your product solves and who it solves it for.

### The Tagline

The tagline is your one-sentence value proposition. It appears in search results and category pages. It does most of the work.

A weak tagline: "An AI-powered tool for content teams."

A strong tagline: "Draft and schedule a month of social content in two hours, without a copywriter."

The strong version names the outcome (a month of content), the time frame (two hours), and the constraint it removes (no copywriter needed). Write your tagline this way.

Formula: "[Specific outcome] for [specific customer type] without [specific friction]."

Or: "[Specific customer type] uses [product] to [specific action] [faster/cheaper/better than alternative]."

Test different taglines across different directories if the platform allows it. Notice which ones drive higher click-through.

### The Short Description (100 to 200 words)

This appears in category listings and below your tagline. Write it in plain language. Address the reader's problem in the first sentence.

Structure:
- Sentence 1: The problem your customer has right now.
- Sentences 2 to 3: How your product addresses it specifically.
- Sentences 4 to 5: Who it is for.
- Sentences 6 to 7: One or two specific features or outcomes.
- Final sentence: A direct call to action.

Example for a reporting tool:

"Most marketing teams spend four to six hours each week pulling data from separate tools and formatting it into reports nobody reads in full. [Product] connects to your existing tools and generates formatted reports automatically, in under five minutes. It is built for marketing teams at companies between 10 and 200 people. Connect Google Analytics, HubSpot, and LinkedIn Ads in one afternoon. Your reports arrive formatted and ready to share every Monday morning. Start your free trial with no credit card required."

### The Long Description (300 to 600 words)

Many directories allow a longer product page description. Use this space to:

- Expand on the problem with specific detail.
- List core features with one-sentence explanations.
- Describe the ideal customer.
- Address common objections or questions.
- Include social proof if allowed (number of customers, specific outcomes, notable companies using the product).

Write this as a flowing description, not just bullet points. Many directories index this text for search. Natural language copy ranks better than a bulleted list.

### Category Selection

Choose your categories carefully. Most directories allow three to five categories.

Pick your most specific relevant category first. If you are a project management tool for marketing agencies, select "Marketing Project Management" before "Project Management." The more specific the category, the less competition and the higher the chance of appearing in filtered searches.

Add secondary categories that represent adjacent use cases. A content calendar tool might list under "Content Marketing," "Social Media Management," and "Marketing Planning."

Avoid irrelevant categories. Padding with unrelated categories hurts your relevance in all of them.

### Screenshots and Media

Screenshots are the most underinvested part of most directory listings. Most products use raw product screenshots that require context to understand.

Better approach:

- Add a headline text overlay to each screenshot explaining what the viewer is looking at.
- Show the outcome, not the interface. A dashboard screenshot with the title "Client-ready reports in one click" is more persuasive than a raw dashboard.
- Include three to five screenshots minimum. Listings with more media get more clicks on most platforms.
- Include a demo video if the platform supports it. A 60 to 90 second product demo video on a G2 or Capterra listing significantly increases engagement.

Use Canva or a design tool to add overlays and polish. Consistent brand colors across screenshots make your listing look professional.

## Part 4: Building a Directory Submission System

Submitting to 50 directories without a system is chaos. Build a tracking system before you start submitting.

### The Directory Tracker Spreadsheet

Create a spreadsheet with these columns:

- Directory name.
- URL.
- Domain rating (check with Ahrefs, Moz, or SEMrush).
- Category.
- Submission type (free, paid, or both).
- Date submitted.
- Listing URL (once live).
- Status (submitted, pending, live, rejected).
- Notes (login credentials location, renewal date if paid, contact if needed).
- Monthly traffic estimate.

Sort by domain rating to prioritize. Submit to the highest-authority directories first.

### Prioritization Framework

Not all directories are worth equal time. Use this framework to prioritize:

**Tier 1 (do first):** Domain rating above 70. Manually reviewed (means a human will see your listing). Known to rank for your category keywords. G2, Capterra, Product Hunt, AlternativeTo, TAAFT, Futurepedia.

**Tier 2 (do second):** Domain rating 40 to 70. Indexed by Google. Reasonable traffic in your category. SaaSHub, BetaList, GetApp, Toolify, Launching Next.

**Tier 3 (batch and do quickly):** Domain rating under 40. Free to submit. Little editorial review. Dozens of smaller directories in this category. Submit in bulk. Expect mixed results.

### Batching Submissions

Block two hours per week for directory submissions. Do not try to submit to all directories in one session. Spreading out submissions over four to eight weeks looks more natural to search engines and keeps you from burning out on a tedious process.

Prepare your assets in advance:
- Product name (exact).
- Tagline (two to three variants).
- Short description (two to three variants).
- Long description.
- Logo (PNG, various sizes: 64x64, 128x128, 512x512).
- Screenshots (labeled, with overlays).
- Product URL.
- Demo video URL (if you have one).
- Contact email.
- Pricing page URL.
- Social links.

Store all of this in a shared folder. You will paste from it repeatedly.

### Password and Account Management

Each directory requires an account. Use a password manager. Store credentials in one place. Many directories send renewal emails or ask you to re-verify listings every 6 to 12 months. If you lose the login, you often lose the ability to edit the listing.

Create a dedicated email address for directory submissions (e.g., listings@yourcompany.com). This keeps the inbox manageable and ensures renewal notices don't get lost in a personal inbox.

## Part 5: Getting Reviews on Review Platforms

A listing on G2 or Capterra with zero reviews does almost nothing. Getting your first 10 reviews is the most important directory task after submitting.

### How to Ask for Reviews

The most effective approach is a direct, personal ask. Do not send a mass email saying "please leave us a G2 review." People ignore this.

Instead:

- After a customer says something positive in a call or email, reply with a direct link to your G2 review form and say: "We'd really appreciate it if you shared that on G2, it takes about three minutes."
- Ask your most engaged users by name. Segment your user base by activity level and email the top 10% with a direct ask.
- Include a review ask in your quarterly customer check-in cadence.

G2 and Capterra both have email campaign tools for review solicitation. G2's "Review Campaign" feature sends automated follow-up emails and provides a link that pre-fills the reviewer's name. This increases completion rates significantly.

### Review Content to Encourage

You cannot dictate what customers write. You can provide context that helps them write a useful review.

When asking for a review, include one sentence like: "If you mention how you use [product] and what's improved since you started, that tends to be the most helpful for other buyers."

This steers reviewers toward outcome-focused language without scripting them.

## Part 6: Product Hunt Strategy

Product Hunt deserves its own section. A successful Product Hunt launch requires preparation, timing, and community support.

### Pre-Launch Preparation

**Build a following before launch day.** Create a Product Hunt account weeks before you launch. Follow others. Comment on products. Build a follower count. Followers get notified when you launch, and notifications drive early upvotes.

**Find a hunter.** A "hunter" posts your product. A hunter with many followers can drive early traffic. Reach out to known hunters in your category and ask them to hunt your product. Alternatively, hunt it yourself if you have an established account.

**Schedule your launch.** Product Hunt resets at 12:01 AM Pacific Time. Products that go live right at midnight have a full 24-hour window. Launch on Tuesday, Wednesday, or Thursday. Avoid Mondays (low traffic) and weekends (very low traffic).

**Prepare your listing before launch day.** Your tagline, description, gallery, and first comment should be ready to post the moment you launch. Do not edit these on launch day under pressure.

**Write a compelling first comment.** The first comment from the maker sets the tone. Write about why you built the product, who it is for, and what you would love feedback on. This is read by many visitors and reviewers.

### Launch Day Execution

Notify your email list, Slack community, LinkedIn network, and Twitter followers the morning of launch. Direct people to the Product Hunt page and ask them to comment and upvote if they find it valuable.

Do not ask for upvotes without context. "Check out our Product Hunt launch and upvote if you think it's useful" converts better than "please upvote us."

Respond to every comment on launch day. Product Hunt rewards engagement. A product with 20 comments and an active founder replies feels alive compared to one with 3 comments and no responses.

## Part 7: Measuring Directory Performance

Track these metrics monthly:

**Referral traffic:** In Google Analytics, look at traffic from each directory domain under Acquisition > All Traffic > Referrals. Note which directories drive actual visits.

**Backlink growth:** Use Ahrefs or Moz to track referring domains. You should see new referring domains added with each batch of submissions.

**Keyword ranking changes:** Track whether your target keywords improve in rank over the 60 to 90 days after a directory submission push. Directory backlinks contribute to overall domain authority, which lifts all rankings.

**Trial starts from directory pages:** If possible, add UTM parameters to your listing URLs (e.g., ?utm_source=g2&utm_medium=directory). Many directories allow you to specify a custom URL. This lets you see which directories drive actual signups, not just visits.

**Review count and rating:** Track the number of reviews and average rating on G2 and Capterra monthly. Set a target: 10 reviews in the first 90 days, 25 reviews in the first year.

## Part 8: Staying Current on New Directories

New directories appear constantly, especially in the AI and agent space. A directory that launches today with low traffic may have 100,000 monthly visits in 18 months.

Set up these signals to find new directories:

- Google Alert for "[your category] directory" and "[your category] tools list."
- Follow Product Hunt's "website builder," "developer tools," or relevant categories to spot new directories launching.
- Watch Indie Hackers for new directory projects in your space.
- Ask customers: "Where do you go to find new tools?" New answers point to new directories.

Claim a listing on any new relevant directory within the first few weeks of its existence. Early listings often get "featured" placement by default and inherit authority as the site grows.
