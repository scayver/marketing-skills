## What This Skill Does

This skill helps you build programmatic SEO programs: the systems that create SEO-optimized pages at scale using templates and data instead of writing each page manually.

Programmatic SEO is appropriate when your market has a large number of related, high-intent search queries that follow a repeatable pattern, and when you have data that can differentiate each page in the series. It is not a shortcut. Done poorly, it produces thin content that Google demotes or ignores. Done well, it builds a durable traffic asset that compounds over months and years.

The question worth sitting with before you start: does each page in your programmatic program genuinely help a specific person with a specific question, or does it exist primarily to capture search traffic?

The answer to that question determines whether your programmatic SEO program will build lasting traffic or get filtered out by Google's helpful content systems.

## What Programmatic SEO Is (and Is Not)

Programmatic SEO is the practice of creating a large number of pages, each targeting a slightly different version of a repeatable search query, using a template-based approach where data populates unique content for each page.

The classic examples:

Zapier built hundreds of thousands of pages targeting queries like "[Tool A] + [Tool B] integration." Each page targets a different combination of tools. The template is the same. The data about each integration populates the unique content.

Nomad List built pages for hundreds of cities targeting queries like "best places to work remotely in [city]." The template is the same. City-specific data (cost of living, internet speed, weather) populates each page with unique, useful content.

Wise (formerly TransferWise) built thousands of pages targeting "[currency A] to [currency B] exchange rate." Each page shows current and historical exchange rate data. The data makes each page genuinely useful. The scale makes it programmatic.

What these programs share: each individual page answers a specific question for a specific person, using real data that makes the page more useful than a page a human would write from scratch without that data.

What programmatic SEO is not: a way to create hundreds of pages with essentially the same copy, swapping out a location or keyword with minimal other differentiation. Google's systems are increasingly effective at identifying and discounting pages that exist to capture search traffic but provide little unique value.

## Identifying Scalable Keyword Patterns

The first step in any programmatic SEO program is finding a keyword pattern that meets three criteria.

Criterion 1: volume at scale. The pattern must have enough search volume in aggregate to make the effort worthwhile. Individual queries in the pattern may have low volume (100-1,000 monthly searches), but the total across all permutations should be meaningful. 500 pages with 500 monthly searches each is 250,000 monthly search impressions at full indexing.

Criterion 2: alignment with your product. The keywords must attract people who have a reason to care about your product. Traffic from people with no purchase intent does not build a business.

Criterion 3: data availability. You need real data to differentiate each page. If you do not have (or cannot acquire) data that makes each page uniquely useful, you will produce thin content.

Common keyword patterns by business type:

Location + service: "[Service] in [City]." Works for businesses with physical presence or location-specific services. A HVAC company, a restaurant reservation platform, a staffing agency. The data source is location-specific information: regulations, pricing, popular options, local statistics.

Tool A + Tool B integration: works for tools that connect to other tools. Each page targets queries like "[Your tool] + [third-party tool] integration." The data source is your actual integration details: what data passes between the tools, how to set it up, what the use cases are.

Alternative to [competitor]: "[Your product] vs [Competitor]" or "Alternative to [Competitor]." Works when you have a large competitive set and enough product differentiation to write useful comparison pages. The data source is detailed, accurate competitive intelligence.

[Role/industry] + [use case]: "[Job title] [software type]" or "[Industry] [software category]." Works when your product serves multiple verticals or job functions. The data source is use case and industry-specific information about how your product applies.

[Location] + [statistic or data]: "[City] real estate prices," "[Country] average salary for [role]." Works when you have access to data others search for. The traffic may not be product-intent traffic, but can drive top-of-funnel awareness for products adjacent to the data.

How to evaluate a pattern before committing to it:

Sample 10-20 individual keyword combinations in the pattern. Plug them into Ahrefs, Semrush, or Google Search Console. Do they have search volume? Do the current ranking pages solve the query well? Is there a differentiation opportunity?

Look at the search result pages for three to five keywords in the pattern. Are the top results programmatic pages from competitors? Programmatic pages that already rank well prove the pattern works. Sites ranking with weak content signal an opportunity.

## Data Sourcing for Page Generation

Your data source determines whether your programmatic pages are genuinely useful or just template-stuffed placeholders.

Internal data sources:

Your product usage data. If your product generates outcomes (reports run, projects shipped, hours saved), you can aggregate and present that data by industry, company size, or location.

Your customer data. Case study data, outcome metrics, industry benchmarks from your customer base.

Integration metadata. If you build integrations with other tools, you have detailed knowledge of how data flows between systems.

External data sources:

Government and census data. Business data from the US Census Bureau, Bureau of Labor Statistics, or equivalent bodies in other countries. This data is free, authoritative, and usable.

Industry reports. Many research firms publish open data that can be used to populate industry-specific pages.

APIs. Financial data APIs, weather APIs, travel data APIs, and others provide real-time or frequently updated data that makes pages dynamically useful.

Web scraped data. Publicly available data scraped ethically from other sites. The legal and ethical considerations here vary by jurisdiction and terms of service.

Crowdsourced data. User-submitted data about locations, tools, prices, or experiences. This requires a community or submission mechanism but produces highly differentiated data.

The data quality threshold: each page in your program needs enough unique data to justify its existence. A page with five data points that are different from every other page in the series is borderline. A page with 20+ unique, relevant data points is defensible.

Before you build the system, build one page manually using the data you plan to use. Is it actually useful? Would someone searching that query be satisfied by this page? If not, your data is insufficient or your template needs redesign.

## Template Design

Your template is the structural framework that every page in your program shares. The template defines what content appears on every page (the constant elements) and where the variable data populates (the variable elements).

Constant elements (same across all pages):
- Site navigation.
- Product description and CTA.
- Explanation of methodology or data source.
- Related pages links.
- Footer.

Variable elements (different on every page):
- The page title and H1 (incorporating the specific keyword combination).
- The data-driven content section (the unique tables, statistics, descriptions, or comparisons for this specific combination).
- The contextual explanation of what the data means for this specific use case.
- Related variations or nearby alternatives (neighboring pages in the series).

The most common mistake in template design: making the variable section too short. A template where the "unique" content is a two-sentence description and a table is almost certainly going to produce pages Google considers thin.

The threshold to aim for: each page should have at least 500-800 words of content that is meaningfully different from other pages in the series, not counting global template elements.

How to write template copy that does not read as template copy:

Write the template prose to avoid triggering obvious pattern recognition. "In [City], the average cost of [service] is [X]" repeated across 500 pages reads as a template. A section that contextualizes the data ("This makes [City] one of the more affordable markets for [service] in [Region], particularly compared to [Nearest Large City]") feels more like a useful guide.

Use natural language conditionals. Your template can include logic that produces different prose based on data values. If cost is above the median, generate one sentence. If below, generate another. This creates variation that is genuinely useful, not artificially randomized.

## Internal Linking Architecture for Programmatic SEO

Internal linking is where most programmatic SEO programs leave significant performance on the table. The pages you create need to pass link equity to each other and to your main site. They also need to be discoverable by Google's crawlers.

Hub and spoke architecture: build category-level hub pages ("Best [service] by city" or "All [Tool] integrations") that link to individual pages in the series. Individual pages link back to the hub. This creates a clear hierarchy that Google can crawl efficiently.

Cross-linking within the series: each individual page should link to related pages in the series. A "Zapier + Slack integration" page should link to "Zapier + Teams integration" and "Zapier + Slack alternatives." These contextual links feel natural to readers and distribute link equity across the series.

Anchor text strategy: use descriptive, keyword-rich anchor text for internal links. Not "click here" but "Zapier + Slack integration guide." Anchor text signals to Google what the linked page is about.

Linkable assets within the series: identify which pages in your series are most likely to attract inbound links. For a location + salary data series, the pages covering major markets (New York, London, San Francisco) will attract far more links than pages covering small cities. Invest more in those high-potential pages.

Sitemaps: submit a sitemap that includes all your programmatic pages. If you are generating thousands of pages, segment your sitemaps by page category to make crawling more efficient.

Crawl budget: large programmatic programs can exhaust Google's crawl budget on your domain, meaning Google crawls and indexes your pages more slowly. Prioritize your most important pages by making them more deeply linked from high-authority sections of your site.

## Avoiding Thin Content Penalties

Google's helpful content system and quality guidelines are the primary risk factor for programmatic SEO programs. Pages that exist primarily to rank (rather than to serve users) are increasingly subject to deranking or deindexing.

The thin content tests to apply to your program:

Does each page answer the specific question someone searching that query would have? Not in theory. In practice. Search the query yourself. What do you actually want to know? Does your page answer that?

Is there meaningful variation between pages? If the only difference between two pages is a city name or a tool name, and the text otherwise identical, they are thin.

Does the page contain data, insight, or content that is not easily found elsewhere? Generic information that Google can find on 50 other pages has low unique value. Data, original research, product-specific knowledge, or synthesized comparisons have high unique value.

Would a content editor at your company be comfortable publishing this page under your brand? If the answer is no, the page should not be published.

The threshold question: if Google deindexed all your programmatic pages tomorrow, would users have lost access to something genuinely useful? If the answer is yes, your program is likely defensible. If the answer is no, your program is at risk.

What to do when some pages in your series are thin:

No-index the thin pages rather than publishing them. Use Google Search Console data to identify which pages in your series receive impressions and clicks. No-index the long tail of pages with zero activity and focus your internal linking on the pages that perform.

Improve the thin pages with additional data, editorial sections, or user-generated content before indexing them.

Consolidate very similar pages. Instead of separate pages for every city in a state, build one state-level page with city sections. Fewer, richer pages often outperform many thin ones.

## Measuring Programmatic SEO Performance

Programmatic SEO programs take longer to show results than editorial content because the pages need to be crawled, indexed, and then earn rankings. Expect a 3-6 month lag from publication to meaningful traffic.

Primary metrics:

Indexed page count: what percentage of your pages has Google indexed? A low indexation rate (below 50% of submitted pages) signals quality issues, crawl budget problems, or sitemap errors.

Impressions by page cluster: use Google Search Console to see impressions by page group. Are your pages appearing in search results? Are impressions growing over time?

Click-through rate: what percentage of impressions result in clicks? A low CTR may signal that your title and meta description are not competitive with other results for those queries.

Organic traffic by page: which individual pages are driving traffic? What do the high performers have in common? Use those patterns to improve lower-performing pages.

Conversion rate from programmatic pages: do visitors from programmatic pages convert to leads or signups? This validates whether you are attracting the right audience.

Leading indicator to watch in the first 90 days:

Indexation rate. If Google is indexing your pages, it has found them and decided they are worth showing users. Indexation rate above 70% is a positive signal.

A question worth examining as you measure: are the pages that drive the most traffic also the pages that drive the most product-qualified visitors? If programmatic SEO is bringing unqualified traffic, that is a strategy problem, not a measurement problem.

## Common Programmatic SEO Mistakes

Publishing before testing indexation: build 10-20 pages first. Submit them. Monitor whether Google indexes them and at what speed. Fix any indexation issues before scaling to thousands of pages.

Insufficient data differentiation: the single most common reason programmatic SEO programs fail. Each page needs real, unique data that makes it more useful than a manually written page without that data.

Ignoring search intent: ranking for a query is only valuable if the people searching that query are potential customers or potential advocates. Building a large programmatic program that attracts the wrong audience wastes resources.

No internal linking strategy: publishing thousands of pages without linking structure means Google discovers them slowly, link equity is distributed poorly, and no individual pages accumulate enough authority to rank.

Building programmatic SEO before baseline content: if your domain has thin editorial content and low authority, programmatic pages will struggle to rank. Build content authority first. Programmatic SEO scales a system that is already working, it does not build one from scratch.

Not monitoring for quality degradation: programmatic programs can degrade over time as the data changes, as products change, or as competitor pages improve. Set a quarterly review to audit a sample of your programmatic pages and update those that have become outdated or low-quality.

Confusing automation with programmatic SEO: using AI to generate hundreds of generic pages is not programmatic SEO. It is automated content production. The distinguishing feature of legitimate programmatic SEO is data: real, unique, useful data that makes each page more valuable than an automatically generated page without it.
